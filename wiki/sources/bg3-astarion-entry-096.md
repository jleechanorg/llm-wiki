The sickening lurch of the transponder, the blinding flash of blue-white light, and then the gut-wrenching sensation of being torn apart and reassembled. Astarion hit the ground hard, the impact jolting every bone in his body, the air knocked from his lungs. He lay there for a moment, blinking, the stench of ozone and burnt demon-flesh fading, replaced by the damp, earthy scent of pine needles and wet soil. Above him, the monstrous Nautiloid, a broken, flaming ruin, careened towards the distant horizon, a fiery comet crashing into the earth, its unholy thrumming finally silenced. They were free. *Free.* A raw, desperate gasp of relief escaped his lips, quickly followed by a grimace as the tadpole behind his eye gave a chilling, insistent squirm. The nightmare was far from over.

He pushed himself up, his muscles protesting but his body miraculously intact. His eyes, cold and calculating, swept his immediate surroundings, then snapped to his companions, scattered and disoriented like discarded toys. The Githyanki landed with a pained grunt, quickly pushing herself to her feet, her golden eyes already scanning the unfamiliar landscape with fierce, unyielding intensity, though a deep gash on her arm still oozed slowly. The burly man, meanwhile, let out a loud groan, clutched at his head, and pushed himself up onto an elbow, his face a mask of exhaustion and lingering shock. His labored breathing was a stark contrast to his usual roaring ferocity.

"Well, that was quite the spectacle, wasn't it?" Astarion drawled, his voice a little shaky, but quickly regaining its sardonic lilt. He strode towards the burly man first, a flicker of pragmatic concern overriding his usual aloofness. "Are you quite finished with your theatrics, brute? We survived, apparently. Though I'm not entirely certain *how*. And you," he turned his gaze to the Githyanki, a subtle nod of grim respect in his eyes. "You seem to attract more wounds than answers. Can you still fight, or are you quite done with the heroics?" He needed them functional. He needed them *now*. His high neuroticism gnawed at him, screaming that the open air meant new, unknown dangers, and he had no desire to face them alone.

"Before we decide whether to *actually* cooperate, or simply watch each other die in a ditch," Astarion continued, a thin, impatient edge to his voice, "perhaps we could address the glaring omission in our introductions. I am Astarion Ancunín. And you two?" He raised a sardonic brow, his crimson eyes fixing first on the Githyanki, then the burly man, awaiting their response with a mixture of disdain and desperate curiosity.

The Githyanki warrior's golden eyes, sharp as a dragon's claw, finally fixed on Astarion. "Lae'zel," she rasped, the single word clipped, devoid of warmth, yet ringing with undeniable authority. She barely spared him another glance before her gaze swept the cliffside, searching, assessing. The burly man, meanwhile, pushed himself fully upright, rubbing his temple. "Gale," he grumbled, his voice rough with lingering disorientation. "And yes, I can still fight, you scrawny elf. Just need a moment to... process. What in the Nine Hells was that thing?" His eyes, still bewildered, flicked towards the plummeting Nautiloid.

Astarion offered a faint, almost imperceptible smirk. *Lae'zel. Gale. How utterly mundane. And predictably direct.* His high neuroticism, however, didn't allow him the luxury of further idle chatter. They were out of the frying pan and into... an entirely new, unknown, potentially more dangerous fire. The salty tang of the sea was now distinct in the air, mingling with the scent of pine and something else, something wild and untamed. The ground beneath his feet was uneven, a rocky, overgrown cliffside overlooking a vast expanse of crashing waves. Pine trees, gnarled and wind-swept, clung precariously to the outcrops, their roots digging deep into the damp earth. The distant roar of the crashing Nautiloid was fading, but the unfamiliar sounds of nature filled the void – the cry of gulls, the endless murmur of the sea, the rustle of unseen creatures in the undergrowth. This was the Sword Coast, or so he surmised from the faint scent of salt and ancient lore, a wild, untamed land utterly unlike the refined, predictable streets of Baldur's Gate. He scanned the tree line, the rocky crevices, the narrow paths leading up or down the cliff face. Every shadow held a potential threat, every rustle of leaves a hidden danger. He was a creature of the city, and this raw wilderness screamed of exposure.
