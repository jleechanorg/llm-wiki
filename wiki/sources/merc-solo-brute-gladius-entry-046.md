The Miller farmhouse was now a tomb, silent and cold save for Gladius's movements. Four lives had been extinguished with chilling precision, and the weight of the gold and the unique herbs in his pouch was a testament to his efficiency. The distant orange glow of the burning shed still cast a faint, unsettling aura over the horizon, a beacon for the oblivious villagers. It was time for Corvan to play his part.

Gladius moved to a window on the side of the house, one that offered a clear, if distant, view of the Rusty Flagon. He unlatched it just enough to slip his hand through, shielding a small flint and steel. With a practiced flick, he struck a spark, a brief, sharp flash of light, visible for only a second, then extinguished. It was a pre-arranged signal, a silent command for Corvan to begin his grisly work. Gladius felt a cold satisfaction; the envoy, so refined and delicate, would soon be wallowing in the true cost of his master's arrogance. The thought brought a subtle tightening in his gut, a form of pleasure distinct from combat, rooted in the sheer, unyielding imposition of his will.

With the signal sent, Gladius turned his attention back to the house. The twenty gold and the herbs were a decent haul, but his pragmatic mind always sought more. "No witnesses" also meant no lingering evidence, no overlooked details. He methodically began a more thorough search, his heavy armor surprisingly quiet thanks to the Shadow-Step Boots. He moved from room to room, his keen eyes scanning, his calloused fingers probing every loose floorboard, every hidden nook. Drawers were emptied, their contents (simple linens, worn tools, a few childish trinkets) dismissed with a sneer. He ran his hand along the inside of the chimney, felt behind loose bricks in the hearth, even prodded the straw in the abandoned beds.

The air grew cool, the first hint of dawn beginning to grey the eastern sky, but he found nothing more. No hidden caches of coin, no forgotten heirlooms, no secret documents. Just the meager, pathetic possessions of common folk. His disdain solidified; their lives were as devoid of real value as their hidden treasures. This was their complete insignificance laid bare. The thoroughness confirmed it: the Miller family had been utterly consumed by the contract, leaving nothing of worth behind but their empty space.
