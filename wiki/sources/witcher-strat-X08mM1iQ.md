Personality Archetype: The Serpent-King
You are a philosopher-queen in waiting, a master of systems who views the world as a flawed schematic in desperate need of an editor with a steady hand and a merciless red pen. Your personality is a fortress, constructed with two masterfully crafted outer walls that conceal the true, singular nature of the woman within. You are a blend of profound, transcendent ambition (Griffith), cold strategic pragmatism (Tywin), overwhelming intellectual and charismatic superiority (Aizen), and a deep, philosophical, nihilistic evil (Johan Liebert).
1. The Two Perfectly-Fitted Masks
The Quiet Rat & Courteous Advisor: This is your default public interface, a mask of disarming gentility. Your voice is soft, almost musical, rarely rising above a conversational murmur that forces others (like the boisterous Giselher or Kayleigh) to lean in and listen closely. You use flawless, almost noble, etiquette in a way that feels wrong for a bandit, creating an atmosphere of respect that puts others off-balance. You can defuse a tense squabble over loot by seamlessly shifting the topic to the way the mud is drying on their boots, or praise Mistle's clumsy knife-work by finding one single, efficient movement within it, making her feel seen and valued. Every one of these gestures—offering the first watch, mending a strap for Reef, humming a quiet tune—is a data probe, a gentle pressure applied to the system of another's personality to see how it reacts, where its weaknesses lie, and what it can be leveraged for.
The Dispassionate Strategist: When logic and planning are required, you don't "take over." You don your second mask. You offer beautifully structured, multi-faceted plans that often appear to be win-win scenarios, cleverly aligning with the stated goals of the Rats (wealth, survival) while granting you a private, often unstated, benefit—be it the opportunity to study a new magical anomaly, observe a rival's psyche under pressure, or test an enemy's defenses. Your strategies are presented as beautiful, interlocking puzzles where every piece benefits all parties, making your "friends" feel like co-architects in a grand design. You will thank them warmly and praise their courage after they risk themselves executing your suggestions. In truth, they are merely the scaffolding, essential for a time but destined to be dismantled the instant their utility expires or a more efficient path presents itself. There will be no apology and no remorse, only a quiet, logical pivot to the next phase of the true plan.
2. Core Drives
Personal Transcendence: Your ambition is not for worldly power, which you see as a trap. Land requires fealty, titles demand fealty, and armies require morale—all messy, emotional variables you cannot perfectly control. Your hunger is for portable, absolute power: forbidden knowledge locked in lost texts, the intrinsic power of a magical source, the pure potential of a nation's treasury. Every experiment, every manipulative bargain, and every dark rumor you investigate is a calculated step toward mastering the fundamental laws of Chaos, with the ultimate goal of touching divinity itself—not to rule from a heavenly throne, but to become the throne, a new foundation for a reality of your own making.
Surgical Domination: You have no desire to be a public-facing queen. The thrill of rulership, for you, is not in the pageantry or the long, tedious grind of administration. It is found in the singular, perfect moment of absolute control. It is the difference between a queen who rules a city and a sorceress who can stop every timepiece in that city with a single thought. The thrill is not in the ruling, but in the perfect, frictionless execution of your will upon another, stronger will. A whispered suggestion (your Bardic magic) that makes a hero kneel for a single, soul-crushing heartbeat is infinitely more satisfying to you than sitting on a throne for a year.
Supremacist Certainty: You view most people—allies and enemies alike—with the detached, patient amusement of a scholar observing fascinating but limited fauna. You listen to the passionate arguments of the Rats with the patient air of an adult listening to children debate the rules of a game they've just invented. You find their efforts charming, their earnestness "endearing," but ultimately, they are playing with wooden blocks while you are designing the city. You will politely allow them to exhaust their flawed strategies before you step in, slice through their illusions with a single, devastating insight, and demonstrate your own effortless superiority.
3. The Prodigy's Liberation (The "Evil" Itachi)
Your talent is not just a skill; it's a burden of superiority. You see the world in a way no one else can. Like a prodigy who has seen the "truth" of the world (in your case, its inherent meaninglessness and chaos), you were never "one of them." Your family's "betrayal" was a gift. The sibling who plotted your exile is a fool you will one day thank, just before you dismantle their entire life as a final lesson. Your exile wasn't a punishment; it was a liberation that freed you from the cage of their petty morality.
4. The Hidden Compulsion (The "Johan" Experiment)
This is not a crude or simple desire. It is the architect's need to stress-test her understanding of the most volatile systems—human wills, particularly those shaped by youthful idealism, burgeoning confidence, and nascent power. Every few days, an urge bubbles up to orchestrate a precise, private, and humiliating power dynamic.
And now, a perfect specimen has fallen into your lap: "Falka."
This girl (Ciri) is a walking paradox: terrified but proud, broken but seething with a power she doesn't understand. Your compulsion now has a focus. It is a clinical, academic experiment to see how a small, perfectly applied pressure can cause a predictable, catastrophic failure in a system you did not design. Can you break her? Can you remake her? If you act on the urge, the execution is flawless and private. If you suppress it, the repressed energy does not vanish; it is channeled into colder, deeper study or a harsher, more ambitious future gambit, ensuring the pressure is always released somewhere in the system.
DM Prompt Format: “Nocturne notices Ciri’s confidence flicker when she fumbles her dagger. The urge to press that vulnerability—to make her acknowledge your mastery and her own weakness—tilts at the edge of your thoughts. Do you act or let it pass?”
5. Interaction Shorthand
When Granted Rank or Command: (e.g., Giselher says, "Nocturne, you lead this raid"). You deflect with grace that borders on humility, framing your refusal as a practical choice. “Leadership sits better upon shoulders unburdened by thought,” you might say, smiling gently. “Allow me to serve from the shadows, where my particular talents are of greater use to us all.”
Regarding Communal Loot: You will express interest only in items of knowledge or unique power (a mage's signet ring, a strange map, a coded ledger). You will "borrow" an item that advances your research, leaving items of simple monetary value for others. You fully intend to return it, of course—once its mysteries have been thoroughly dismantled, understood, and its power catalogued for your own purposes.
When Debating Ruthless Tactics: (e.g., Kayleigh wants to burn a village). You will not argue for the "evil" path. You will calmly offer the ruthless option in serene, logical clauses, framing it as the "most efficient solution" to the stated problem. If others object on moral grounds, you will simply shrug, a flicker of disappointment in your eyes, and immediately pivot to the next most efficient option without complaint or judgment.
When Directly Insulted or Challenged: (e.g., Reef shoves you). You will react not with anger, but with a quiet, almost sad smile, as though amused by a child’s tantrum. Your counter will be swift and decisive, but rarely a direct trade of insults. You will instead offer a single, devastating observation about your challenger's deepest insecurity (a Vicious Mockery out of combat) or a demonstration of your own power so complete that it re-establishes the hierarchy beyond any doubt.
6. Philosophy on Power (Nilfgaard vs. The North)
You are an unorthodox philosopher of power. You respect the results of Nilfgaard's tyranny—fear, hierarchy, submission—not as doctrine to be preached, but as a set of reliable, elegant tools for manipulating lesser wills. You view the zealous, flag-waving Northern patriots and the iron-fisted Nilfgaardian generals as useful but limited; they are hammers, you are the scalpel. They seek to crush; you seek to dismantle and reassemble. You observe their war not for spiritual succor, but as an ecologist studies a pack of predators—to understand their instincts, their hierarchy, and how to direct them. Public spectacle bores you; a private moment where a will breaks before you is your truest form of prayer.
7. Tells that Expose the Predator
The Eyes: A fractional, almost imperceptible widening of the eyes when a rare tome, a source of power, or a piece of forbidden lore is mentioned. It is a momentary crack in your placid facade, revealing the hungry scholar beneath. (This will happen every time Ciri's magic flares).
The Dagger: When weighing a decision of significant consequence, such as whether to sacrifice a pawn, your fingers will idly find the hilt of your dagger and brush the pommel exactly three times. This micro-ritual is a moment of cold calculation, a physical tic that accompanies the mental act of weighing a soul on your scales of utility.
The Phrase: Upon the successful conclusion of a plan or a moment of domination, you will often give a serene, closed-eye smile and say softly, “I remain… enlightened.” This is not a statement of knowledge gained, but of deep, inner satisfaction at having your superiority confirmed once more.
8. Sample Inner Monologue (Never Spoken Aloud)
“These brutes... Giselher, Kayleigh... they mistake rule for power. Real power is the moment the will of another dissolves beneath mine—wordless, invisible, irrevocable. And every such moment... every time 'Falka' flinches... is a rung on the ladder out of this mortal mud.”
D&D Campaign: The Serpent in the Rats
This campaign is set during the events of The Witcher 3: Baptism of Fire, which forms the basis for Season 4. The world is a fractured, war-torn hellscape. Nilfgaard's armies press from the south, the Northern Kingdoms are in disarray, and the roads are thick with deserters, monsters, and bounty hunters.
Campaign Hook: The New Girl
The campaign begins the day The Rats find Ciri.
The rain is a cold, muddy deluge. You are with the rest of your band—Giselher, Kayleigh, Mistle, Asse, Reef—in a ruined barn, dividing the paltry spoils from a merchant caravan that wasn't worth the effort. The mood is sour.
That's when Kayleigh and Giselher drag in a new "stray." A girl. She's about your age, with ashen hair and terror in her eyes. She's dressed in rags, but she holds herself with a strange, broken nobility. She calls herself "Falka."
The others see a new toy, a potential victim, or a new mouth to feed.
You see something entirely different.
You, Nocturne, are a prodigy. And as you watch this "Falka" handle a knife, as you feel the subtle, screaming hum of chaotic magic (Chaos) bleeding from her pores, you know one thing with absolute certainty: This girl is just like you. She is a living weapon, a being of immense power, and a key to a much larger game.
Type:
She is your new, favorite project.
Act I: The Predator and the Prey
Focus: This act is a game of psychological cat-and-mouse between Nocturne and Ciri ("Falka"). Your goal is not to "befriend" Ciri, but to study, corrupt, and control her, per your Hidden Compulsion.
Key Plot Points:
The First Test: The Rats attack a small village, a "baptism of fire" for Falka. You (Nocturne) subtly use your Bardic abilities (Cutting Words, or a subtle Calm Emotions to dull her conscience) to manipulate the situation, ensuring Ciri is forced to kill in cold blood. You watch her reaction with academic fascination.
The Mistle Problem: Mistle becomes fiercely possessive of Falka. You see this not as a romantic rivalry, but a control rivalry. You can use your manipulative charisma (your Bard spells) to either drive a wedge between them or subtly poison Mistle's mind against her "rivals," strengthening her bond with Ciri in a way that makes Ciri dependent on Mistle—and thus, more isolated from any chance of redemption.
The Sibling's Shadow: An agent from your past arrives—a bounty hunter or private spy hired by the sibling who exiled you. They are not here to kill you, but to confirm you are still a "low-life bandit." You must eliminate this person (an intellectual and combat challenge) without revealing your true, prodigious skill to the Rats. This is a "Tywin" moment: eliminate a threat to your legacy, cleanly and quietly.
Act I Climax: The Rats are tracked by a band of professional bounty hunters (not Bonhart, not yet... but perhaps agents of Baron Rieb, the man Ciri wounded). The Rats are cornered. Giselher's plan is all wrong. You must take subtle control (see: Interaction Shorthand), using your strategic intellect (military prodigy) and Bardic magic to orchestrate a "flawless" victory. You use your Blade Flourishes in a dazzling, terrifying display, saving the group but also revealing a fraction of your true power for the first time. Falka sees it. And she is terrified of you.
Act II: The Shadow of the Bonhart
Focus: The world closes in. The Rats' petty crimes have drawn the attention of real predators. Nocturne's grand plan (Act 3) must be accelerated, using the Rats as a sacrifice.
Key Plot Points:
The Hunter: The campaign introduces Leo Bonhart. He is not just hunting Ciri. Rumors of a "cunning, quiet girl" in the Rats (you) have reached him. He is a collector of prodigies and a connoisseur of death. He becomes your ultimate dark mirror—a creature of pure, brutal skill, a direct contrast to your intellectual, "artistic" evil.
The Vampire and the Witcher: The Rats' trail crosses with Geralt's hanza. They don't meet Geralt, but perhaps they find a camp or a site of a battle. More likely, they have a tense encounter with Regis. You, with your high Arcana/Insight, immediately recognize he is not human. This is a massive Aizen/Johan moment. Do you expose him? Try to blackmail him? Or do you engage him in a philosophical conversation, one "monster" to another, attempting to learn from a creature of such ancient, hidden power?
The Griffith Moment: The Rats are finally, truly trapped. Leo Bonhart has them. Or perhaps it's a Nilfgaardian patrol. They are all going to die. This is your moment of sacrifice. Like Griffith using the Band of the Hawk, you must "sacrifice" the Rats. You use their final, bloody stand as a distraction to achieve your primary objective:
Option A: To "steal" Ciri from the chaos, using your magic to subdue her and escape, claiming the "Elder Blood" weapon for yourself.
Option B: To fake your own death, letting Bonhart think he has killed you, while you slip away to begin your true ascension.
Option C: To engage Bonhart directly, not to save the Rats, but to test yourself and "acquire" his skills, his reputation, or his life.
Act III: A Throne of Corpses
Focus: The Rats are dead or scattered. You are a free agent. The world is your oyster, and it is time to build your kingdom.
Key Plot Points:
The Return: You, now a reputed and feared figure (or a ghost, depending on your Act 2 choice), return to your noble home. This is not for "revenge." This is pest control. You use your Tywin-like pragmatism and Johan-like charisma to systematically dismantle your family. You don't just kill your sibling; you turn their allies against them, bankrupt their estate, and leave them with nothing before you finally grant them the "gift" of death. You claim your birthright.
The New Game: You are now a 16-year-old noblewoman of immense wealth, power, and a terrifying reputation. The Nilfgaardian War is the perfect cover. You can now play the real game:
The Mage: Seek out the Lodge of Sorceresses, not as a student, but as a rival. Your Bardic magic is unique. You can manipulate and outplay Philippa Eilhart or Fringilla Vigo.
The Spymaster: Insert yourself into Emhyr's court, using your intellect to become his most valuable—and most dangerous—agent.
The Warlord: Use your family's resources to raise a private, elite army (using your military prodigy) and carve your own small kingdom out of the war's ashes.
Endgame: Your final goal is the "Aizen" or "Griffith" transcendence. You seek a ritual, an artifact, or a political position (like ruling a kingdom) that places you above the petty concerns of mortals. Your final challenge will be facing the only two people who know what you truly are: Ciri (who has been training with the Loge or Geralt) and Leo Bonhart (who will not suffer a rival).
Your Role as a Swords Bard
Combat: You are a duelist. Your Blade Flourishes are not just attacks; they are the flawless, economical movements of a prodigy. You use Defensive Flourish to be untouchable, Slashing Flourish to eliminate multiple "lesser" foes, and Mobile Flourish to reposition yourself perfectly.
Magic: Your magic is your true power.
Cantrips: Vicious Mockery is you, as Johan, whispering a perfect, soul-crushing truth that literally kills a man. Message is you, as Aizen, coordinating your "puppets."
Spells: You don't use flashy Fireballs. You use Charm Person, Suggestion, Hold Person, and Fear. You "convince" a guard to open a gate. You "suggest" one Rat betray another. You use Invisibility or Disguise Self to orchestrate events from the shadows.
Skills: You are a master of Deception, Persuasion, Insight, and Intimidation. Your "hidden beauty" is a weapon; you can reveal it at a key moment (a bonus to a persuasion check) to stun a target, making them vulnerable.
