You step into the center of the muddy courtyard, the heavy iron keys of Ashwood clinking at your belt like a jailer’s promise. At your command, your retainers drag the crates of looted gear from the armory into the light. You move among the twenty Shield-Breaker veterans, signaling your men to unlock their shackles. One by one, the 'Anvil' and his survivors stand, rubbing their bruised wrists and eyeing you with a mixture of suspicion and raw, desperate hunger for the steel you are returning to them.

'You were discarded by Malakor,' you rumble, your voice amplified by the Gryphon Helm's visor, carrying across the yard to where Gratian’s Eaglets stand watching. 'He sent a Shadow-born to leash you and left you to rot in this mud when the arithmetic changed. But I do not waste assets. I offer you a path back to the only life you know: the hunt.'

You take up a heavy, steel-shod shield and thrust it into the hands of a scarred veteran. 'We ride for Black-Hollow. We ride to excise the rot of the Covenant from this province. Killing Malakor will not just be an act of Imperial justice; it will be the forging of your new legend. Serve me, and you will not be slaves in a mine—you will be the Imperial Anvil, heroes whose names are whispered in Aeterna as the men who broke the Unchained.'

The silence that follows is thick and suffocating. You have given them back their swords, but you have failed to give them back their pride. The veterans look from the gear to 'The Anvil,' then back to you. There is no cheer, no 'volunteer' surge from the overhearing Eaglets. Instead, a low, cynical mutter ripples through the ranks. They take the steel because they are soldiers and they have no other choice, but their eyes remain dead, fixed on the mud. You have re-armed a unit that still feels like a collection of ghosts, and the Eaglets watching from the battlements only tighten their grip on their pikes, sensing the instability of your new 'vanguard.'

You turn to the quartermaster, emptying your heavy purse of the remaining 559 gold pieces. 'Everything,' you command. 'Every scrap of whetstone, every spare potion, and the finest oil for my breastplate. Then, spend the rest on these wretches. If they are to be my shield, I want them wrapped in something better than rags.' By the time the sun begins to dip, your gold is gone—every last groat—replaced by the glint of sharpened edges and the medicinal smell of alchemical draughts. You are 'all in,' as you planned, but you are leading a vanguard of men who look like they are marching to their own funeral rather than their glory.
