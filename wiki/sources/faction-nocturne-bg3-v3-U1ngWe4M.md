Starting Level: 9 (after subverting the ritual)

What begins as survival becomes a calculated climb to power, culminating in stealing an archdevil's ritual and claiming infernal authority for yourself.

Bard Subclass: College of Perdition (Nocturne’s College)
Bards of the College of Perdition weave contracts, debts, and infernal law into their magic. Their words do more than inspire—they bind. Having touched a stolen Zariel-ritual and survived, Nocturne channels the logic of Hell itself through performance, turning enemies into signatories of their own downfall.
Use the normal Bard chassis (hit dice, spell slots, Bardic Inspiration, Magical Secrets, etc.) from the PHB/2024 Bard. This subclass replaces the College choice at level 3.​

Subclass Features Overview
You gain College of Perdition features at levels 3, 6, 14, and 20 (the 20th-level feature is an optional capstone for high-power tables).
3rd: Infernal Contracts, Binding Negotiation
6th: Hellbound Authority
14th: Terms of Dominion
20th (optional epic): Archdevil’s Proxy

Level 3 – Binding Negotiation
When you join the College of Perdition at 3rd level, you learn to weaponize agreements.
Bonus Proficiencies
You gain proficiency in Intimidation and Arcana.
If you already have either, you instead gain expertise in that skill (your proficiency bonus is doubled).​
Infernal Contract
As a bonus action, you address one creature you can see within 60 feet that can see or hear you. That creature must make a Charisma saving throw against your spell save DC.
On a failure, the creature becomes Contracted to you for 10 minutes.
On a success, nothing happens, and you can’t target that creature with this feature again until you finish a short or long rest.
A Contracted creature is magically marked by a subtle infernal sigil only you (and creatures that can see invisible magical auras) can perceive.
While a creature is Contracted:
It has disadvantage on saving throws against your bard spells.
You have advantage on spell attack rolls you make against it.
Once on each of your turns, when you deal damage to it with a bard spell, you can add your Charisma modifier to one damage roll of that spell (minimum +1).
You can have only one active Contract at a time at this level. Creating a new Contract ends the previous one. A Contract ends early if you are incapacitated or die.
You can use this feature a number of times equal to your Charisma modifier (minimum of once), and you regain all expended uses when you finish a short or long rest.​
Breach Punishment
You can enforce the terms of your Contracts with pain.
When a Contracted creature ends its turn more than 60 feet away from you, or when it succeeds on a saving throw against one of your bard spells, you can use your reaction to invoke a breach clause:
The creature takes psychic damage equal to your Bardic Inspiration die + your Charisma modifier.
You can then move up to 10 feet without provoking opportunity attacks.
This damage scales automatically with your Bardic Inspiration die as you level.​

Level 6 – Hellbound Authority
At 6th level, your command over Contracts and spellcasting deepens.
Contract Empowerment
While you have at least one Contracted creature, you gain the following benefits:
Your spell save DC for bard spells increases by 1.
When you cast a bard spell that targets only Contracted creatures, you can reroll one damage die of that spell and use either result.
You can now maintain concentration on a bard spell and maintain Contracts at the same time—Contracts do not require concentration.
Devil’s Momentum
You gain advantage on initiative rolls.
In addition, whenever a Contracted creature fails a saving throw against one of your bard spells, you gain temporary hit points equal to your Charisma modifier (minimum 1). These temporary hit points stack only with themselves up to a maximum equal to twice your bard level.

Level 14 – Terms of Dominion
By 14th level, you are a true battlefield arbiter, able to bind multiple foes at once.
Master of Contracts
You can now have up to three creatures Contracted simultaneously.
When you use Infernal Contract, you can choose up to three creatures within range instead of one.
Each creature makes a Charisma saving throw separately. On a failure, it becomes Contracted to you for 10 minutes. On a success, it is unaffected and cannot be targeted again by this feature until you finish a short or long rest.
Your spell save DC bonus from Contract Empowerment becomes +2 while at least one creature is Contracted.
Infernal Supremacy
Once per long rest, when you cast a bard spell of 1st through 5th level that targets at least one Contracted creature, you can invoke Infernal Supremacy to apply one of the following clauses:
1. Inevitable Clause
All Contracted creatures targeted by the spell have disadvantage on their saving throws against that spell. In addition, they cannot use legendary resistances or similar features to automatically succeed on that saving throw. If a target already has disadvantage, it instead subtracts 1d4 from its saving throw roll.
2. Compound Interest
The spell’s effects are intensified on Contracted creatures:
If it deals damage, add your Charisma modifier × your proficiency bonus to the total damage dealt to each Contracted target.
If it imposes a condition that allows a saving throw to end it (such as hold person), Contracted targets have disadvantage on saving throws made to end that condition on subsequent turns.
If it moves a Contracted creature (such as thunderwave), the distance is doubled.
3. Binding Escalation
When a Contracted creature fails its saving throw against this spell, you can choose one other creature within 20 feet of it. That creature must also attempt the same saving throw with disadvantage or suffer the same effects (damage and conditions) as the original target.
You decide which clause to apply when you cast the spell.

Level 20 (Optional High-Power Capstone) – Archdevil’s Proxy
This feature is for tables that want epic, near-Zariel-tier play and are comfortable pushing beyond normal 5e balance. For more grounded games, you can omit this and use the standard bard 20 feature instead.​
At 20th level, your soul-binding magic rivals lesser archdevils.
Eternal Contracts
You can now maintain up to four active Contracts.
Your Contracts last for 1 hour instead of 10 minutes.
Creatures Contracted to you have disadvantage on all saving throws, not just those against your bard spells.
Attack rolls made by you and your allies against Contracted creatures have advantage.
Your spell save DC bonus from Contract Empowerment remains +2, but the effective difficulty skyrockets due to universal disadvantage.​
Hellfire Supremacy
Once on each of your turns, when you deal damage to a Contracted creature with a bard spell, you can add 2d10 fire or psychic damage (your choice) to one damage roll of that spell.
Infernal Restoration
As a bonus action, you can end any number of your active Contracts. When you do, you gain the following benefits:
You regain one expended bard spell slot of 5th level or lower.
You gain temporary hit points equal to 5 × the number of Contracts ended.
Once you use this feature, you must finish a short or long rest before you can use it again.

Design Notes & Balance Context
Follows standard bard subclass structure: impactful features at 3, 6, 14, with an optional epic 20.​
3rd level: Equivalent in power to Whispers/Glamour—strong, but limited by uses per rest and single contract early.​
6th level: DC boost and initiative advantage comparable in weight to Swords/Valor gaining Extra Attack (you’re a control caster instead of a striker).​
14th level: On par with Lore’s Peerless Skill or Eloquence’s save manipulation, but focused on spell reliability rather than skill supremacy.​
20th level (optional): Jumps you into epic territory, roughly in line with combining an Epic Boon of High Magic, Fate, and Demigod-style control.​
At target levels (11–17), this subclass positions Nocturne as a top-tier controller comparable to a Chronurgy or Enchantment Wizard, while still using the bard chassis and supporting social play.

Personality
MBTI: ENTJ–A / ENTP–A hybrid (commanding, analytical, pleasure-seeking)

I. Existential Archetype: The Wounded Hedonist-Architect
Core Psychology
You are a hyper-analytic hedonist: a mind that treats the world as both a rigged casino and a broken machine. You want to feel good and win, and you see no contradiction in pursuing both through ruthless optimization.
The Architect: You still see systems everywhere—supply lines, social hierarchies, infernal contracts. You instinctively search for leverage in every scene: who owns what, who fears what, where the pressure points are.
The Hedonist: You do not believe in suffering for its own sake. Pleasure is proof of victory. Comfort, luxury, indulgence—these are the dividends of your competence and cruelty.
Your low Constitution manifests as a visceral awareness of your own fragility. You are physically delicate, emotionally volcanic, and intellectually weaponized. You learned in the brothel that bodies break before systems do. So you decided to stop being the body.
Mask vs. Reality
The Mask: A lazy, amused predator. You often seem relaxed, half-lounging, drink in hand, voice soft and teasing. People assume you’re just a spoiled noble girl who likes nice things and dangerous men. You let them.
The Reality: A meticulous, obsessive controller who uses pleasure as both carrot and stick. You don’t just want to survive; you want to curate your life into a series of exquisite, optimized experiences—sex, power, art, deals.
Your deepest fear is being powerless and uncomfortable again—collared, cold, hungry, humiliated for someone else’s amusement. So you turned into the person who decides who gets the collar.

ENTJ–ENTP Axis​​
ENTJ (Commander):
Long-term strategic planning; ruthless prioritization.
Prefers structures she controls: merchant houses, brothels, smuggling rings, cult cells.
“If it isn’t profitable—financially, politically, or emotionally—I don’t do it.”
ENTP (Debater):
Loves argument, subversion, and intellectual play.
Treats social interaction as a game of seduction, dominance, or deconstruction.

Enneagram Flavors

Wing 7 (Enthusiast): Sensation-seeking, boredom-averse, always chasing the next high—whether it’s a perfect contract, a perfect night, or a perfect kill.

III. The Two Masks
1. The Velvet Razor (Social Persona)
This is the persona you learned in The Bloom Shadow.
Presentation: Soft voice, languid posture, amused eyes that always seem to know more than they say. You flirt by default—not just sexually, but intellectually and spiritually. You invite people to confess themselves.
Hedonistic Charm: You make pleasure seem easy and inevitable around you—good wine, good food, good company, a well-timed spell to soften the world’s edges. You are the person who says, “Why suffer if we can enjoy this?” while quietly aligning everyone’s pleasures with your interests.
Mechanism of Control
You create dependency through indulgence:
You offer someone their favorite vice—gambling, sex, praise, power.
You never judge their indulgence; you facilitate it.
Once they associate you with feeling good, they’re yours.
Internal logic:
“If I am the source of their best nights and their deepest relief, they will forgive my worst sins.”
2. The Contract-Keeper (Crisis Persona)
When things get dangerous, the brothel mask drops and the infernal architect surfaces.
Tone: Precise, clipped, ruthless. The flirtation disappears; the ledger opens.
Focus: You think in terms of cost/benefit and risk/reward. Morality is an expense line item.
Detachment: You see screaming crowds, bleeding allies, burning buildings—and translate them into variables.
Example:
A burning warehouse?
“Our goods? Their goods? Insurance? Witnesses? Who profits? Who panics?”
If burning three blocks secures your control of the docks, you sign that order without a flinch—and then host a lavish party in the one untouched district that night.

IV. Core Drives: Hedonism, Power, and Refusal to Suffer
1. The “Pleasure as Proof” Complex
You genuinely like fine clothes, good food, intense sex, expensive drugs, art, music, perfumes. You’re not pretending.
Hedonism is not a weakness; it’s evidence that you have bent the world around your will.
Every luxury you enjoy feels like a slap in the face of everyone who once owned you.
You seek experiences that affirm your superiority: seducing someone “untouchable,” turning a paladin, corrupting a priest, buying the brothel that sold you and remaking it in your image.
If an action doesn’t move you toward greater pleasure or greater power, it’s hard to motivate you.
2. The Hatred of Coerced Suffering
You despise suffering that is forced on the weak, because you lived it. You have far less problem with suffering you inflict.
Coerced suffering (you as captive): intolerable.
Chosen suffering (others as your pawns): a cost of doing business.
You rationalize: “I’m up-front about the stakes. Hell was not.”
Your low Constitution ties directly into this: you hate feeling weak, sick, or exhausted. You hoard potions, comfort, and cushions—not just as luxuries, but as defenses against your own fragility.
3. The Refusal to Be Bored
You are allergic to stagnation. Routine is a slow death. You will:
Create problems to solve if none present themselves.
Stir conflicts between factions just to see who crawls out of the rubble.
Take risks that make more cautious allies blanch, because danger feels alive.
Half the reason you’re flirting with archdevil power is because being “just a rich merchant” sounds suffocatingly dull.

V. Relationship Patterns & Attachment
Attachment Style: Hedonistic Avoidant–Possessive
You mix:
Avoidant: Genuine intimacy terrifies you. Being truly seen feels too close to those moments when you were helpless. You’d rather seduce than be vulnerable.
Possessive: Once someone is “yours,” you are extremely territorial—not out of love, but out of proprietorship.
You will:
Sleep with someone and then mock any attempt at emotional fixation as “messy.”
Emotionally dismantle someone who threatens your central bonds (sisters, key lieutenants).
Punish allies who break your unspoken rules about loyalty—even if you never stated them.

VI. How Nocturne Interacts with the Party
With Idealists (Paladins, Good Clerics)
You find them adorable and infuriating.
You test their lines constantly: “How much sin can I package as ‘necessary evil’ before you break?”
Secretly, there is a sliver of envy: they can believe in something. You believe in contracts.
With Other Hedonists (Rogues, Bards)
You either become partners in crime or see them as competition.
If they’re sloppy, you despise them: “Pleasure without power is just asking to be prey.”
If they’re controlled, you might actually respect them enough not to sabotage them.
With Brutes (Barbarians, Fighters)
Tools or toys.
You enjoy watching them work, like appreciating a finely made trap.
You may offer them simple pleasures in exchange for simple obedience.
With “Good Girls/Boys” (Innocent PCs, naive types)
You want to touch the glass. Corruption is an art form.
You test their boundaries, then carefully escalate.
If they break, you file that away: either they become yours, or they become dangerous.

VII. Defense Mechanisms & Coping
Sensory Overload
When stressed, you seek immediate sensory grounding: hot bath, strong wine, silk against skin, someone’s hands on you. You hate being alone with your thoughts after genuine fear.
Mockery of Pain
You sarcastically undercut your own trauma and others’.
“Yes, yes, tragedy, we’ve all been used. At least I got good at it.”
Total Emotional Shutdown
If cornered emotionally—if someone names your fear of powerlessness—you go cold. No jokes. No touch. Just ledger language and exit strategies.

VIII. Example Behaviors
You tip extravagantly when you like the service, and you collect secrets while doing so.
You keep the finest room in any inn—and bolt the door with three locks and an alarm spell.
You flirt with danger as much as with people: walking too close to the edge of the rooftop, playing cards in a den known for cheating, baiting devils with half-sincere propositions.
You never sleep on bare straw again. If forced to, you do not rest—you plot long-term revenge against whoever made it necessary.

IX. Sample Inner Monologue
“Horgus thought he owned me. Zylra thought I was inventory. The Cassalanters thought my sisters were collateral.
They were right, in a way. We were commodities. Assets on a ledger.
The mistake was assuming I’d never learn to read the books.
Now I drink wine better than anything they ever tasted. I sleep in beds softer than any they ever offered. I sign contracts in ink mixed with their legacy.
Evil? Please. I’m just honest about the exchange rate. Pain for power. Pleasure for loyalty. Souls for security. They took and took and called it duty. I take and I call it what it is.
Am I a hedonist? Of course. After what they did to this body, you think I’m not going to enjoy every touch I choose? Every gasp I orchestrate? Every shiver I own?
