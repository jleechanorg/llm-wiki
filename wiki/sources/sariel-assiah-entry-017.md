The polished marble of the corridors blurred beneath your feet as you almost ran, fleeing the suffocating silence of Valerius's study. The air in the Spire felt suddenly thin, choked with unspoken judgments and the phantom whispers of "The Architect of the Fall." You clutched your mourning robes, shrinking inward, desperate for a sanctuary where the cold logic of your brothers could not reach.

Cressida’s chambers, usually a vibrant space filled with scrolls and a faint scent of exotic perfume, offered a small respite. The door stood ajar, and inside, your closest friend sat at a writing desk, a pile of diplomatic correspondence before her. She looked up as you entered, her sharp eyes, usually so calculating, immediately softening with concern as she took in your trembling posture and tear-stained face.

"Sariel?" Lady Cressida Valeriana (Level 12 Expert, Age 20) rose swiftly, her chair scraping softly on the floor. In a single fluid motion, she closed the distance, her hand reaching out to gently grasp your arm. Her touch was warm, solid, and utterly devoid of judgment. Her elegant robes, always meticulously pressed, now felt like a comforting anchor. "What happened? You look as though you've walked through a ghost."

You sagged against her, the carefully constructed facade of composure you had tried to maintain shattering completely. The dam broke. "They hate me, Cressida," you choked out, the words ripped from your raw throat. Your voice was thick with shame and a profound, aching grief. "Both of them. Cassian... he looked at me like I was a wound. And Valerius... he said I was 'inefficient.' A flaw in their calculations." Hot, fresh tears streamed down your face, blurring the soft focus of her chambers. "It's true. I told him. I screamed. She died because of me, Cressida. I broke her." You buried your face against her shoulder, the warmth of her presence a fragile comfort against the desolate cold within. You had confessed your deepest guilt, and for a moment, the crushing weight of it eased, if only slightly.
