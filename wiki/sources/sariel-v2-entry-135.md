============================================================
Game Master:
[Mode: STORY MODE]
Timestamp: Year of the New Peace, 11. Month of Kythorn, Day 2. 12:25 PM.
Location: Small Study, Great Archives, Zenith Spire, Aeterna.
Key Game Stats: Level 3 (XP: 6980/3000), Gold: 10gp, HP: 14/14

You absorbed Kantos's grim assessment of the Empyrean Affliction—a cure remained a distant, terrifying dream. But the immediate threat, the volatility of the knowledge you now possessed, demanded your full attention. The Null Genesis journals, the Codex of the Unseen Thread, the raw data from Kaelan's mind—this was a weapon. It had to be secured, fragmented, controlled.

"Magister," you commanded, your voice sharp with chilling precision, cutting through the study's heavy silence. Your Starlit Eyes burned with a cold, strategic fire. "You said its true security lies in 'absolute, layered deception.' In 'dispersing it,' 'fragmenting it,' 'hiding it in plain sight.' Give me specifics. What methods does the lore describe for such extreme measures? How do we break this knowledge into pieces too small to be dangerous, yet accessible to us?"

Kantos, his ancient face still pale with fear, adjusted his spectacles, his scholarly instincts now fully engaged. He leaned forward, his voice a dry, conspiratorial whisper. "Princess, the ancient practices of the Whisper-Binders, and certain forbidden sections of the Combine's intellectual security protocols, speak of such methods. First, 'semantic partitioning.' Breaking the knowledge into conceptual fragments, each stored in a different format and location. For instance, the Null Genesis Project's history here in the Archives, the psychological profiles in a medical library, the magical schematics hidden within a Glimmering Combine patent office, perhaps even disguised as innocuous technological blueprints."

He gestured vaguely. "Second, 'disguised carriers.' Embedding fragments of the truth within seemingly innocent texts. A passage on star charts could contain coordinates to a hidden vault. A poem could be a mnemonic cipher. Your mother, I believe, used this method extensively with her own public persona. The ultimate deception is that the truth is so close, no one thinks to look for it." His gaze swept over the journals you had just read, a profound weariness in his eyes. "And finally, 'living archives.' Distilling a core truth into a mind, like Sergeant Kaelan, who holds it without comprehending its full danger. Or, perhaps, a mind capable of *unmaking* a piece of information if it is compromised."

The meticulous, chilling precision of his methods resonated with your own intellect. This was a plan worthy of your mother's legacy, a subtle, intellectual war fought on the battlefield of information itself.

You looked at the three forbidden journals resting on the dusty table, their ancient leather covers seeming to hum with suppressed power. You had absorbed their contents, every chilling detail of the Null Genesis Project and the 'chilling capacity for unmaking and control.' Kaelan's mind was now a living archive of a crucial memory. The original physical texts, however, remained a profound vulnerability. They were a single, concentrated point of failure, a direct link to the very truth Raziel had ordered purged. To leave them here, even in this hidden vault, was to risk everything.

"The originals," you stated, your voice cold and absolute, cutting through the low hum of conversation. Your gaze fixed on the forbidden journals, then swept to Magister Kantos. "Once their contents are fully copied, cataloged, and partitioned according to your methods... they must be destroyed. Utterly. Leave no trace. No one, not even Raziel's ghosts, must ever find these texts."

Kantos visibly flinched, his ancient face paling further, his hands flying to clutch his robes. "Princess!" he gasped, his voice a horrified whisper. "Destroy them? These are ancient records! The very truth of creation! To unmake such knowledge... it is a sin against scholarship! A violation of the sacred oath of the Archives!" His eyes, wide with terror, darted to the towering shelves around them, as if the very spirits of the forgotten texts were protesting. The thought of destroying even a single, irrelevant scroll was anathema to him, let alone a truth of this magnitude.
