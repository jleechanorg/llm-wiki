Astarion let out a soft, exasperated sigh as the Githyanki offered her unhelpful insights. "Fine," he muttered, more to himself than to her. "Brute force it is, eventually. But only after we've exhausted all *intelligent* options." He turned from the stubbornly impatient pair, his gaze drawn to the pulsating, organic heart of the Nautiloid. The infernal engine. It was a grotesque marvel, a fusion of technology and biology that defied logic, yet it hummed with a terrifying power. If they truly wanted to escape, perhaps they needed to understand *where* they were going, or better yet, wrest control of their grotesque prison.

He approached the massive, throbbing mass with a mixture of revulsion and cold, detached curiosity. The air here was hotter, thick with the smell of ozone, burnt flesh, and something acrid, almost like battery acid mixed with blood. Tendrils of living sinew snaked across the floor, connecting to panels of unidentifiable, glowing goo and intricate, alien circuitry. He ran a gloved hand (or what felt like it, as his clothes were still clinging to his body) over a slick, chitinous surface, feeling the thrumming vibrations beneath his palm. His mind, usually sharpest when dissecting the subtle lies of nobles or tracing a path through the urban sprawl, now grappled with this utterly alien logic. He looked for a pattern in the pulsing lights, a sequence in the shifting membranes, anything that hinted at its function beyond simply propelling them through the void. The omnipresent hum of the tadpole behind his eye intensified here, almost harmonizing with the engine's thrum, a chilling reminder of his own impending transformation. He felt a wave of nausea, quickly suppressed. He would not become a Mind Flayer's pet.

He crouched, examining a series of larger, fleshy tubes that pulsed with an internal, sickly green light, feeding into a central vortex. Was this power? Navigation? He focused, pushing past his visceral disgust, searching for any exposed conduits, any interface that might be familiar, even in its alienness. The urge to simply smash it was strong, but that would only trap them, or worse. He needed information, a weakness, a lever.

The Mind Flayers were absorbed in this, their very being attuned to its operation, but you are not. You understand nothing of its function beyond its horrifying propulsion. A cold wave of frustration, mixed with a deeper fear of the unknown, washes over you.
