Inside the small alcove, the air is thick with the scent of ozone and the heavy, cooling tension of a world-shifting revelation. 

Crixus stands by the stone table, his massive shoulders slumped, his eyes fixed on the Aegis of Julia where it rests against the limestone. He doesn't look like the 'Undefeated Gaul' who roared at the pits of Capua; he looks like a man who has just realized he was trying to cage the sun. 

"Despair is a cold bedfellow," Crixus rasps, his voice a low, gravelly vibration that barely carries over the wind. "I have seen men in the pits who welcomed the gladius because they could no longer bear the weight of the sun. But those men... they trembled when the steel touched their skin. They feared the pain, even if they craved the end. Despair makes a man brittle. It makes him slow."

He turns his head, his gaze meeting Naevia’s with a pained, searching intensity. "The boy didn't tremble. On those Spires, he didn't look like a man seeking an exit. He looked like a man holding a door. I called it Roman arrogance because it was easier to hate than to understand. But a boy of sixteen summers... to carry such skills? To strike with the weight of a god and the silence of a tomb? I have killed veterans of twenty campaigns who did not have half his poise."

Spartacus steps forward, the light catching the sharp, clinical lines of his face. He looks at the tunnel where Aurelia vanished moments ago. "Perhaps it is not a death wish," the Thracian muses, his voice a resonant anchor. "Perhaps it is a humility so deep it looks like madness to men like us. We have fought to own our lives, Crixus. He seems to be the only man in the Republic who believes his life is the only thing he truly *doesn't* own. He treats his blood like a currency he is obligated to spend for others."

Spartacus looks back at Crixus, a grim understanding passing between the two leaders. "A level of mastery like his... at sixteen? It is not normal. Even the Miasma of his bloodline cannot explain the discipline in his hands. He is truly brave, not because he wishes to die, but because he refuses to let anyone else die in his place. We were wrong to treat him as a wolf. He is the blade we prayed for, and we have been using him to sharpen stones."

Crixus offers a singular, sharp nod—the final surrender of his antagonism. "The penance is done. If he wants a grave, he will have to wait until Rome is ash. Bring him back, Aurelia. Tell the boy... tell the specialist... that the mountain has seen enough of his sweat. It's time we see his steel at the head of a line that means something."

Outside, the freezing mist continues to swirl, but the heavy, unnatural silence of the grotto has finally broken, replaced by the quiet, efficient preparations for a war that will now have a Sun at its center.
