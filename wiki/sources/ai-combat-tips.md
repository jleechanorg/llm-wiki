# AI Combat — Getting Better Outcomes

Combat in AI RPGs is not primarily about dice rolls. It's about the quality of your description and how well you've established context before the dice fall.

---

## Description > Dice

The AI responds to what you describe, not just what you roll. A vague action gets vague results; a specific action gets specific consequences.

**Weak:** "I attack the goblin."

**Strong:** "I feint high with my sword and slash at its exposed left leg as it overextends forward."

The detailed action gives the AI more to work with. It also lets you guide the narrative toward interesting consequences — the goblin trips, its weapon clatters away, it cowers.

---

## Context Is Everything

Before a fight, establish:

- Your positioning and your enemy's positioning
- Your equipment and its relevant properties
- Your companion's location and action (if applicable)
- Any environmental factors you want the AI to use

"We have the high ground and the enemy is funneling through a narrow bridge. My companion Mira is circling to flank. I'm using a flail — I want to aim for gaps in armor."

This context gives the AI material to make the combat vivid and mechanically interesting.

---

## The Flank Isn't Automatic

Many AI RPG systems will grant advantage or extra damage for flanking. But the AI won't automatically know you're flanking unless you establish it narratively and mechanically.

State your intent: "I'm flanking from the left — the enemy has to choose between me and my companion." Then describe the tactical result: "It turns to face me, exposing its back to Mira."

---

## Use Environment

Don't fight in a featureless void. Establish the terrain:

- Uneven ground that could cause a trip
- Furniture that could be used as improvised weapons
- Cover positions
- Light sources and shadows
- Chokepoints and escape routes

"I kick the table over to create cover between us and the archers" gives the AI permission to generate consequences around cover, archery suppression, and the table's collapse.

---

## Companion Autonomy in Combat

Companions who act autonomously in combat generate emergent narrative. They also sometimes do unexpected things.

**Direct control:** You write their actions. Consistent but predictable.

**Autonomous:** The AI decides. Surprising but occasionally inconsistent.

**Hybrid:** Give them a clear directive and let the AI execute. "Mira circles to keep the enemy between us at all times — she's playing defense while I press forward."

The directive gives the AI a behavioral anchor. The autonomy gives you surprise.

---

## After the Fight

Don't let combat end with "the enemy is dead." Direct the aftermath:

- What does the enemy yield? Information, gear, a secret?
- What did this fight reveal about the larger threat?
- Is anyone watching? Does news spread?

The best combat encounters generate story threads that outlast the fight itself.

---

## When to Avoid Combat

Sometimes the most strategic third option is not fighting at all. Before committing:

- Can you identify what the enemy actually wants?
- Is there a resource you have that they need?
- Could you use the environment to make combat favor you without engaging?
- Who else is affected by this fight's outcome?

"I talk to the bandit leader first" is not weakness. It's information gathering before committing. Sometimes the talk reveals a third option.
