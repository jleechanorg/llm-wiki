The Stonehand dwelling was now Gladius's domain, a sealed chamber where the warmth from the dying hearth and the scent of cooking oats created a false sense of security for the oblivious occupants. The muffled shouts of the distracted couple outside faded, their simple curiosity a pathetic distraction. Gladius felt the cold, precise satisfaction of an unchallenged predator, the raw, immediate pleasure of his perfectly set trap.

His hand, large and calloused, held the Silent Strangler’s Cord, a dark, lethal whisper in the dim light. He had already located them: three more souls, likely children and an older relative, separated from the main couple by a hanging cloth. Their lighter, quicker breaths, their unconscious shifts, painted a picture of utter vulnerability. His mind, unburdened by empathy, focused purely on the efficiency of the kill. This was the final act of this contract, the last note in a brutal symphony of erasure. The sun, a faint promise of orange and purple on the horizon, would soon illuminate the full extent of his chilling work. Their simple existence, their pathetic slumber, was about to become currency in his own ascent.

He stood in the dim, hearth-lit common room, the quiet broken only by the crackle of the fire and the distant, fading shouts of the Stonehand couple. *Three inside,* he mused, his gaze fixed on the hanging cloth that screened their slumber. *Two outside.* The thought of simply eliminating them all now, quickly, cleanly, was efficient. But efficiency wasn't the only pleasure. These Stonehands, strong-backed, loud, so convinced of their simple, honest strength... they offered a different kind of prey.

A subtle, cold hum began to resonate within Gladius, a deeper, more insidious satisfaction unfurling. *What if I kill two, and take one child hostage?* The idea slid into his mind like a perfectly honed blade. It wasn't about necessity; it was about the art of it. The exquisite cruelty of it. He pictured the terror, the desperation in the eyes of the child’s parents when they returned, drawn back by the lure of their offspring, only to find themselves utterly helpless. It would be a tangible display of his control, bending their stubborn, simple strength into pathetic, quivering weakness. He would make them *see* their insignificance, make them *feel* it, before he snuffed them out. It was a refinement of the "no witnesses" clause, a subversion of their expected defiance. He would not just kill them; he would break them first. The thought of their dawning horror, the realization of their utter powerlessness, was a rush, a raw, immediate experience of absolute domination, a performance for himself.
