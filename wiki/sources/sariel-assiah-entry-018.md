Your confession ripped through you, leaving you hollow and exposed. You clung to Cressida, burying your face in the soft silk of her shoulder, your body wracked with shuddering sobs. The scent of her expensive perfume, usually a symbol of the polished court, was a grounding anchor in the storm of your grief. You felt her hand stroke your hair, a gentle, comforting rhythm. It was the first true physical comfort you'd received since Mother's death, and it made the dam break even further.

As your sobs quieted to ragged gasps, a desperate, raw question clawed its way out of your throat, barely audible against the hammering of your heart. You pulled back just enough to look at her, your tear-filled eyes pleading. "Do you... do you blame me, Cressida?" Your voice was thin, fragile, laced with the terror of a final, crushing judgment. "Do you think I'm responsible? Like them?" You gestured vaguely towards the Spire, towards your brothers' silent accusations. "Tell me the truth. Please."

Cressida’s sharp eyes, usually so quick to assess a political advantage, held only a fierce, protective tenderness. Her gaze was steady, unwavering. She reached up and gently brushed a damp strand of hair from your face, her touch firm.

"Sariel, look at me," she commanded softly, her voice crisp but kind. You reluctantly met her gaze. "No," she stated, her voice absolute, leaving no room for doubt. "I do not blame you. And neither would your mother." Her jaw tightened imperceptibly as she spoke of Alexiel. "The warlord Mordan," Cressida continued, her tone now hardening, subtly shifting the focus from your culpability to his villainy, "is a monster. He is a master of psychological warfare. He hunted you, he captured you, and he used every vile method he possessed to break you. No one, Sariel, could have withstood that. Not even your brothers, for all their bluster."

She paused, her gaze sweeping your face, as if willing you to understand. "Your mother knew Mordan's methods. She knew the trap he set was designed to exploit her deepest conviction. She went into that valley knowing the risks, knowing the choice she might have to make. That was her decision, Sariel. Not yours. She saved you, and those children, because that was her nature. That was her choice. Not your fault."

Her words, so firm and clear, were a lifeline in the swirling darkness of your guilt.
