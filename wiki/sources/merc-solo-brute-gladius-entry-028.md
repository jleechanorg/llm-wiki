Gladius left Corvan a trembling, broken shell in the dim common room of the Rusty Flagon. The taste of Corvan’s utter terror, the triumph of forcing such compliance, lingered in his mind, a far more satisfying reward than any gold. The sun was now fully up, casting pale light across Greenmeadow, making the thought of immediate action inefficient and risky. "No witnesses" meant working under the cloak of darkness. It was time to prepare.

He moved silently, thanks to the Shadow-Step Boots, slipping away from the inn and deeper into the marshy outskirts of the village. He found a secluded copse of gnarled, ancient trees, their roots snaking through the damp earth, offering a perfect, hidden bolthole. The air here was cool and still, damp earth and faint, sweet marsh gas replacing the stale ale of the tavern. He shed his greatsword, leaning it against a tree trunk, its heavy presence a silent sentinel. His splint mail, usually a rigid confinement, felt like a second skin as he settled down, not for sleep, but for a deep, focused period of rest and mental calibration.

His low intelligence might suggest a simple mind, but Gladius's thoughts were a singular, brutal calculation: the coming night's work. His eyes, though closed, saw the Miller farmhouse, the Blackwood cottages, the Stonehand dwelling. He ran through the layout, picturing entry points, escape routes, the paths to the marsh for disposal. The Silent Strangler's Cord felt cool against his fingers as he ran them over it, mentally rehearsing the swift, quiet choke. He envisioned the faces of the peasants, their futile struggles, the grim satisfaction of their demise. This wasn't about hate; it was about efficiency, about demonstrating absolute, unyielding power. He let his instincts guide him, his muscles relaxing, his EP pool (now refreshed to a full 16) a potent reservoir of his own concentrated will. The anticipation of the hunt, the promise of utter control, was a heady, almost erotic pleasure. He would be ready when darkness fell.
