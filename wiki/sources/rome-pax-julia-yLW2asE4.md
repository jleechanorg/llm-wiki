A Complete Campaign Guide
In the Shadow of a Dying Republic, an Empire of Blood and Prophecy is Forged, and the Sun of Egypt Rises Over the Seven Hills of Rome.
The world knows the story of Rome: a city of laws and legions, of senators and conquerors. But this is not that world. This is the world that was born on the Ides of March, 44 BCE, when the conspirators’ knives were stayed and the will of one man, Gaius Julius Caesar, was allowed to fully reshape the destiny of humanity.
Haunted by the Fated Miasma, a hereditary connection to the chaos of destiny itself, Caesar did not just conquer the Republic; he remade it. His ambition was not merely for power, but for a stable reality, a single timeline free from the maddening whispers of what could be. To achieve this, he shattered tradition, embraced the divine, and looked not to Rome's past, but to the ancient, god-haunted shores of the Nile.
This is the story of the dynasty he created, a line of philosopher-kings and warrior-queens, a fusion of Roman iron and Egyptian mysticism. It is the story of an empire that reached for the heavens, forever trying to outrun the prophetic shadows that nipped at its heels.

World History: The Julian-Ptolemaic Century (44 BCE - 56 AD)
This timeline diverges completely from our own at the moment of Caesar's ultimate triumph.
The First Generation: The Age of the God-King (44 BCE - 14 BCE)
44 BCE: The Day of Ascension. The plot to assassinate Caesar fails. Guided by a powerful vision, Caesar preemptively strikes, exposing the conspiracy in the Senate. The conspirators are arrested, and in the ensuing panic and popular outrage, Caesar is granted emergency powers. The Republic is suspended.
43 BCE: The Legitimization. Caesar shocks the world. He formally divorces his Roman wife, Calpurnia, and marries Queen Cleopatra VII of Egypt. He adopts his biological son with her, Ptolemy Caesar, and renames him Gaius Julius Caesarion. He uses his unprecedented power to pass the Lex de Filio Aegypto, formally legitimizing Caesarion and making him a Roman citizen and his undisputed heir. This act outrages the conservative aristocracy but is wildly popular with the plebeians, who are captivated by the glamour and immense wealth of the Egyptian queen. Rome's treasury is flooded with Ptolemaic gold.
42 BCE: The Assassination of Octavius. The young Gaius Octavius, Caesar's grand-nephew, becomes the figurehead for the disgruntled Republican remnants. Seeing him as a legitimate threat to his son's succession, Caesar's agents, likely under the command of a loyal Mark Antony, arrange for the boy's "unfortunate accident." The death of Octavius extinguishes the last hope of the old guard.
40 BCE - 30 BCE: The Wars of Unification. Caesar and Antony lead the legions on a series of final campaigns. They crush the last Pompeian remnants in Spain and then turn their attention to Parthia. The campaign is a triumph, avenging Crassus and securing the eastern frontier. During this time, Cleopatra acts as regent in Rome, her intelligence and administrative skill proving formidable. She begins to subtly introduce Egyptian culture and religious ideas into the capital.
29 BCE: The Divine Triumph. Caesar returns to Rome and celebrates an unprecedented triumph. He is officially deified by the Senate as Divus Julius, and he begins construction of a magnificent temple to Venus Genetrix, the mythical ancestor of his line, and another to the goddess Isis. Rome begins to look less like a Latin city and more like a cosmopolitan, Hellenistic capital.
14 BCE: The Passing of the Eagle. Caesar dies of old age, having ruled as a living god for three decades. He leaves behind a unified, powerful, and culturally transformed Roman Empire. The principle of primogeniture is established. The throne, and the full, terrifying burden of the Fated Miasma, passes to his only son.
The Second Generation: The Age of the Sun-Prince (14 BCE - 37 AD)
14 BCE: The Accession of Caesarion. Imperator Caesarion Augustus ascends to the throne. Raised by a Roman general and an Egyptian queen, he is a man of two worlds: charming, intelligent, multilingual, and a patron of the arts and sciences. He is also more deeply afflicted by the Fated Miasma than his father, his visions more vivid and chaotic. His mother, the Dowager-Empress Cleopatra, remains his most trusted advisor.
14 BCE - 10 AD: The Golden Age. Caesarion's early reign is a period of peace and unparalleled prosperity. He uses Egyptian wealth to fund massive public works across the Empire. Alexandria becomes a second capital, a global center for learning and scholarship, rivaling Rome itself. The syncretic Cult of Serapis-Jupiter becomes the de facto state religion, blending Roman and Egyptian beliefs.
10 AD - 25 AD: The Shadow of the Miasma. As Caesarion ages, the prophetic curse deepens. He becomes more erratic and reclusive, haunted by visions of betrayal and cosmic dread. He relies heavily on his brilliant but ruthless Praetorian Prefect, Sejanus, to manage the daily affairs of the Empire. This period sees the rise of a powerful secret police, the Frumentarii, who begin to silence dissent with brutal efficiency.
26 AD: The Britannic Mystery. Driven by a particularly terrifying vision of a "drowned king" and a "serpent in the fog," Caesarion orders a reconnaissance expedition to the mysterious, fog-shrouded isle of Britannia. The expedition vanishes without a trace, and the event deepens the Emperor's paranoia.
37 AD: The Storm on Capri. Caesarion, now a deeply paranoid and isolated figure, retreats to his palace on the isle of Capri. He is found dead after a violent storm, his chambers destroyed as if by a powerful, unseen force. The official cause is a stroke, but rumors persist that he was consumed by his own prophetic power, his mind finally shattering under the strain of the Miasma. He leaves behind twin children to inherit a now-troubled throne.
The Third Generation: The Age of the Dragon Twins (37 AD - 56 AD)
37 AD: The Twin Heirs. Caesarion's will splits the succession between his twin children, Julia Drusilla and Gaius Germanicus, known to the world as Caligula. They are to rule jointly. Both have inherited the Julian curse, but it manifests in them in terrifyingly different ways. Julia is plagued by visions of the past, seeing ghosts and historical echoes. Caligula sees only the future, a maddening torrent of chaotic possibilities that fuels his megalomania.
37 AD - 41 AD: The Reign of Chaos. The joint rule is a disaster. The twins, bound by blood but divided by their visions, are locked in a struggle for control. Their court is a whirlwind of decadence, cruelty, and political instability. The Empire, for the first time in a century, begins to show signs of fracture as the siblings' contradictory orders confuse provincial governors and legionary commanders.
41 AD: The Death of the Sister. Julia Drusilla dies suddenly, allegedly of a fever. Caligula is devastated, but also liberated. He declares his sister a goddess and consolidates his sole rule over the Empire. His behavior becomes even more erratic. He wages a mock war against the sea, declares his horse a consul, and demands he be worshipped as a living god, Neos Helios.
42 AD - 55 AD: The Tyranny of the Seer. Caligula's reign is one of terror, but also one of strange, prophetic brilliance. He uses his visions to preempt assassination plots with terrifying accuracy, executing senators before they can even voice their treason. He spends the treasury on bizarre, massive projects that seem insane, but which are guided by his attempts to avert the futures he sees in his visions.
56 AD (Present Day): The campaign begins. The Emperor Caligula is at the height of his madness but also his power. He has just announced his most ambitious and terrifying project yet: a full-scale invasion of Britannia, the isle that haunted his father. He is not seeking conquest; his visions have told him that the key to controlling—or perhaps ending—the Fated Miasma lies in the sacred groves of the Druids. The Roman world holds its breath, ruled by a mad seer, as the legions prepare to march into the mists at the edge of the world.

First Generation Campaign: The Age of the Eagle (c. 60 BCE - 14 BCE)
This campaign follows the rise of Gaius Julius Caesar as he conquers the Republic and marries a queen, forging a new kind of empire.
Playable Characters: Caesar's Inner Circle
Gaius Julius Caesar: The visionary. Your ambition is driven by the need to create a stable world to quiet the prophetic chaos in your mind.
Marcus Antonius (Mark Antony): The loyal soldier. Your fist and your most reliable commander, a man who lives for battle and the spoils of war.
Queen Cleopatra VII: The ambitious partner. You are not a mistress, but a co-conspirator. A brilliant ruler in your own right, your goal is to secure the future of your Ptolemaic dynasty by binding it to the rising power of Rome. Your knowledge of statecraft, poison, and eastern mysticism is an invaluable asset.
Titus Labienus: The Republican conscience. Caesar's most brilliant legate, a man torn between his loyalty to his commander and his love for the dying Republic.
Plotline 1.1: "The Alexandrian Gambit"
Theme: Alliance, Intrigue, and Seduction.
The campaign begins not in Rome, but in Alexandria during the civil war. Caesar has pursued Pompey to Egypt, only to find him assassinated. The players are embroiled in the Alexandrian civil war between Cleopatra and her brother. This act is a mix of urban warfare, political maneuvering, and a high-stakes romance. Cleopatra must win Caesar's support, not just with her charm, but by proving her political and strategic value. Antony and Labienus must lead Caesar's outnumbered legions in vicious street-to-street fighting against the Alexandrian army. The act culminates with the defeat of Cleopatra's brother, the famous burning of the Library of Alexandria (perhaps to destroy records that could harm Caesar or Cleopatra), and the forging of their powerful, world-changing alliance.
Plotline 1.2: "The Rubicon of the Mind"
Theme: The Point of No Return & The Rejection of Tradition.
The players return to Rome victorious, but Caesar faces immense political opposition for his affair with a foreign queen and his increasingly king-like ambitions. The conservative Optimates, led by Cato, use this to turn the Senate against him. This plotline is a political cold war. Cleopatra uses her vast wealth to win over the Roman people with games and grain shipments. Antony uses his veterans to control the streets. Caesar, plagued by visions of his own assassination if he follows the traditional path, must make a decision. The climax is not a river crossing, but a speech in the Senate. He formally announces his intention to marry Cleopatra and legitimize their son. He dares the Senate to oppose him, backed by the implicit threat of his legions. This act shatters centuries of Roman tradition and solidifies the battle lines for the future. Labienus, unable to accept a Roman general becoming an eastern-style monarch, breaks with Caesar, becoming a future enemy.
Plotline 1.3: "First Man in Rome"
Theme: Purge, Power, and the Forging of an Empire.
The Senate's opposition crumbles into a final, desperate assassination plot. This plot fails, just as in the main history. But the aftermath is different. Caesar, with the full support of a furious Cleopatra and a vengeful Antony, does not offer clemency. He initiates a brutal and systematic purge of his enemies, the Night of the Long Knives. The players must now become the architects of an authoritarian state. Cleopatra helps design a new system of administration based on the efficient Ptolemaic bureaucracy. Antony oversees the military purges. Caesar, now free from his primary rivals, begins his great wars of consolidation against Parthia and Germania, securing the borders of his new world-state. The campaign's first generation ends with Caesar as an undisputed, god-like ruler, but one who has had to sacrifice the last vestiges of the Republic and his own moral compromises to achieve it.

Second Generation Campaign: The Age of the Sun-Prince (c. 14 BCE - 37 AD)
This campaign follows the reign of Caesar and Cleopatra's son, a man of two worlds, as he navigates the golden age and the growing darkness of his inheritance.
Playable Characters: The Alexandrian Court
Imperator Caesarion Augustus: You are the Emperor, a philosopher-king raised with the best of Roman and Greek education. You are haunted by the Fated Miasma, but you try to use your visions to guide the Empire towards prosperity and enlightenment.
The Dowager-Empress Cleopatra: Your mother, your regent in all but name. She is the power behind the throne, her political acumen and vast network of eastern spies protecting you from threats you do not see. She fears the Miasma that is slowly consuming you.
Marcus Agrippa: Now the aging, veteran commander of the entire Roman military. He was loyal to your father, and he has transferred that loyalty to you, seeing you as Caesar's son. He is a rock of Roman tradition in your increasingly Hellenistic court, often clashing with your mother over policy.
Lucius Aelius Sejanus: A brilliant and ambitious equestrian whom you have elevated to the rank of Praetorian Prefect. He is your right hand, ruthlessly efficient and utterly loyal to your power. He is the man who does the dirty work, managing your spy network and enforcing your will while you focus on grander projects.
Plotline 2.1: "The City of the World's Desire"
Theme: Utopia-Building & The Seeds of Corruption.
The campaign opens during the height of your reign. Your goal is to complete your father's work and create a stable, prosperous world. This plotline focuses on grand projects. You might travel to Alexandria to oversee the construction of a new, even greater Library, or to Rome to build magnificent temples and aqueducts. But beneath the surface, problems fester. The Roman aristocracy resents the growing influence of your mother and your "foreign" ways. Sejanus brings you constant reports of plots, and you must decide whether to act with your father's ruthlessness or with a more merciful hand. Agrippa warns that the legions are growing soft in this new age of peace and luxury. The players must balance the dream of utopia with the practical, often brutal, realities of power.
Plotline 2.2: "The Serpent in the Fog"
Theme: Cosmic Horror & The Unseen Enemy.
A disturbing vision begins to plague you, Caesarion. It is a recurring nightmare of a mist-shrouded island, a wicker man burning against a grey sky, and a serpent coiled around a standing stone. You become obsessed with it. You dispatch a covert expedition of your best Frumentarii, led by a trusted commander, to the mysterious isle of Britannia to investigate. This plotline is a tense, long-distance mystery. The players receive fragmented reports from the expedition, which speak of strange cults, eerie fog, and ancient ruins that seem to defy Roman engineering. The reports become more and more frantic, until they stop altogether. The entire expedition is lost. This failure deeply unnerves you, planting a seed of true fear. You realize there are powers in the world that even your prophetic visions do not fully understand.
Plotline 2.3: "The Storm of the Fates"
Theme: Paranoia, Betrayal, and the Shattering of a Mind.
The loss of the Britannic expedition breaks something in you. The Fated Miasma, once a tool, becomes a torrent of terror. Your visions become unreliable, showing you betrayal in every corner. You retreat to your palace on Capri, trusting no one but Sejanus. This act is a tense, paranoid court intrigue. Agrippa tries to manage the Empire in your absence. Your mother, Cleopatra, fears Sejanus is isolating you for his own ends and begins to plot against him. The players are trapped in a web of their own making. Do you trust your mother, who sees your Prefect as a threat? Do you trust Sejanus, who brings you daily proof of plots against your life, even from within your own family? Your paranoia escalates until the climax: a violent, supernatural storm engulfs Capri. The campaign's second generation ends with your death, not at the hands of an assassin, but seemingly consumed by a psychic storm, your mind finally lost to the chaos of the Fates you tried to control.

Third Generation Campaign: The Age of the Dragon Twins (c. 37 AD - 56 AD)
This campaign is a high-octane psychological thriller, a story of two cursed heirs ruling a world that fears them.
Playable Characters: The Mad Court
Imperator Gaius Germanicus (Caligula): You are the Emperor, but your mind is a raging sea of possible futures. You see a thousand ways for the Empire to fall every single day. Your erratic, cruel, and grandiose behavior is a desperate, ongoing attempt to perform a mad dance, constantly changing the steps to avert the apocalypses only you can see. You are not just a tyrant; you are the sole, exhausted guardian against a future of infinite terror.
Diva Julia Drusilla: Your twin sister, but you are not dead. You are a player character who has chosen a different path. Horrified by the Miasma, which for you manifests as seeing the ghosts of everyone your family has killed, you have retreated from public life. You have joined the forbidden Vestal Virgins, seeking purification and a way to sever your connection to the Fates through ancient rituals. You are your brother's secret confidante, the only person who understands his madness, and you try to guide his visions from the shadows of the temple.
Cassius Chaerea: A veteran Centurion of the Praetorian Guard. You are an old soldier, a man of simple duty who served under Caligula's heroic father, Germanicus. You are personally loyal to the Emperor but increasingly horrified by his cruelty and instability. You are torn between your oath and your conscience.
Aulus Plautius: The most competent and respected general in the Roman army. You are a pragmatist who cares only for the stability of the Empire and the welfare of your legions. You see the Emperor as a dangerous liability but recognize that deposing him could trigger a civil war that would be even more destructive. You serve him, hoping to mitigate the damage his madness can cause.
Plotline 3.1: "The Divine Audience"
Theme: Megalomania & The Performance of Power.
The campaign begins with Caligula at his most infamous. He demands the Senate deify him as a living god, Neos Helios. This plotline deals with the political and social fallout. The players must manage the crisis. Caligula must use his terrifying foresight to root out the senators plotting against him, making examples of them in the arena. Julia, from her temple, must use her influence with the other religious cults to smooth the transition, perhaps by blending the imperial cult with that of Isis or Mithras. Chaerea must reluctantly carry out the Emperor's brutal orders, his disgust growing with each execution. Plautius must keep the legions on the frontiers from revolting at the news of their Emperor's blasphemy. This is a dark, challenging plotline about how to serve a ruler who is both a monster and the only thing holding the world together.
Plotline 3.2: "The Serpent's Egg"
Theme: The Quest for a Cure & A March into Madness.
Caligula becomes obsessed with what happened to his father's expedition in Britannia. His visions tell him that the island's Druidic priests hold the secret to controlling, amplifying, or even severing the Fated Miasma. He announces his intention to conquer the island. This act is the preparation for a massive invasion. Plautius is given command and must assemble the invasion force, a huge logistical and strategic challenge. Chaerea is tasked with training a new Praetorian cohort specifically for the "unconventional warfare" they will face. Julia's visions of the past give them clues, showing them echoes of her father's lost expedition and the strange, powerful magic they encountered. Caligula himself oversees the plan, his strategic orders a bizarre mix of tactical genius and seeming insanity, all driven by his prophetic attempts to counter the druids' own powers of foresight.
Plotline 3.3: "Into the Mists"
Theme: Invasion, Cosmic Horror, and The Final Confrontation with Fate.
The Roman invasion of Britannia begins. This is the campaign's finale. It is not a standard military conquest. The moment the legions land, they are beset not just by British warriors, but by the island's strange, reality-bending properties. The fog is unnatural, causing soldiers to get lost and experience hallucinations. Time seems to flow differently in the ancient groves. The Druids are not simple priests; they are powerful seers who have their own connection to the Fates, and they can counter Caligula's visions, turning the war into a blind, terrifying struggle between two sets of prophets. The players must lead their forces into the heart of the island, to the sacred isle of Mona (Anglesey). The climax is a confrontation between Caligula and the Arch-Druid. It is not a physical battle, but a psychic duel, a war of visions fought in the swirling chaos of the Fates themselves. The campaign could end in several ways:
The Severance: Caligula succeeds, severing the Julian line from the Fates. He is cured, but he is now just a man, a tyrant without his divine insight, vulnerable and likely to be assassinated soon after.
The Ascension: He masters the Miasma, absorbing the Druids' power. He becomes a true, stable god-king, his rule now absolute and eternal, a terrifying but peaceful end for the world.
The Shattering: The psychic duel is too much. Caligula's mind shatters completely, and the backlash of prophetic energy unleashes a wave of chaos across the Empire, triggering a new age of civil war and uncertainty. The players have failed, and the dynasty ends in fire.

Excellent choice. The Pax Julia is a superb name. It's strong, evocative, and immediately establishes the central premise of your alternate history: this is a world where "peace" is synonymous with the absolute, unquestioned rule of a single family. It's a name that is both a promise and a threat, dripping with the kind of authoritarian grandeur that defines this version of Rome.
Let's formally integrate The Pax Julia into the setting and detail how it shapes the world's culture, politics, and language.

The Name of the World: The Pax Julia
(The Julian Peace)
The known world is no longer referred to by the patchwork of names of the old Republic. In the wake of Caesar's ascension and the end of the civil wars, the entire dominion under Roman rule is officially and colloquially known as The Pax Julia.
The Philosophical & Cultural Impact of the Name:
1. The State's Propaganda:
For the Julian regime, the name is the ultimate tool of statecraft. It accomplishes several things at once:
It Re-defines "Peace": The term rewrites the definition of peace (pax) from a state of non-war to a state of absolute order under Julian authority. Any dissent, rebellion, or political opposition is therefore not a disagreement, but an act against peace itself—a crime against the entire world.
It Justifies a Monarchy: It subtly but powerfully argues that true, lasting peace is only possible through the singular will of the Julian line. The chaos of the late Republic is contrasted with the stability brought by Caesar. The message is clear: the Republic means civil war; the Julians mean peace.
It Is Inherently Dynastic: By tying peace to a family name, it lays the groundwork for hereditary rule. The peace is not the Pax Romana (the Peace of Rome), which belongs to the state; it is the Pax Julia, which belongs to the family. To ensure peace, one must ensure the continuation of Julian rule.
Common Phrase: "For the sake of the Peace..." (A common justification for any act of authoritarianism, from raising taxes to executing political rivals.)
Official Inscriptions: New buildings, aqueducts, and monuments are not just inscribed with SPQR (The Senate and People of Rome), but with a new imperial stamp: SPQR • PAX JULIA.
2. The Perspective of the Governed:
For the Roman Citizen: For the average plebeian in Rome, the name is a blessing. The generation before them knew nothing but civil war, proscriptions, and chaos. Caesar and his heirs brought stability, grain, grand building projects, and spectacular games. They have traded their political freedom for security and bread, and most consider it a bargain.
For the Provincial: For the people in Gaul, Hispania, or Greece, the Pax Julia is a more complex reality. It means an end to the endless, predatory wars of Republican governors, replaced by a single, ruthlessly efficient administration. It means safety from barbarian raids but also the heavy hand of Roman tax collectors and the imposition of Roman law. It is a peace bought with their autonomy.
For the Aristocracy: For the old senatorial families, the name is a bitter pill, a daily reminder of their subjugation. They live under "The Julian Peace," which means their ambitions are permanently capped, their political power neutered, and their lives are subject to the whims—and prophetic paranoia—of the ruling family.
3. The Heretic's View:
Those who know the secret of the Fated Miasma see the name for what it truly is: a desperate attempt to impose order on a chaotic reality. The Pax Julia is not a political state; it is a metaphysical project. It is the name of the stable timeline that Caesar and his heirs are trying to forge.
The Goal: To make the Pax Julia so absolute, so unchanging, that it silences the maddening visions of what "could be." Every law passed, every legion marched, every enemy executed is another brick in the wall of their stable reality.
The Fear: The greatest threat to the Pax Julia is not a foreign army, but a new prophecy, a powerful vision, or an uncontrolled outbreak of the Fated Miasma that threatens to show them a future where their "peace" shatters.
How this affects the campaign:
Moral Ambiguity: The players are not just fighting for power or conquest. They are the architects and enforcers of the Pax Julia. Every mission, whether it's putting down a rebellion or assassinating a rival, can be justified as an act "for the sake of the Peace." This creates a rich ground for moral conflict. Are they heroes bringing order to a chaotic world, or are they tyrants crushing freedom for the sake of their own sanity?
Campaign Arcs: The three generations of the campaign can be framed around the state of the Pax Julia.
Generation 1 (Caesar): Forging the Peace. The bloody, difficult work of creating the Pax Julia from the ashes of the Republic.
Generation 2 (Caesarion/Octavius alternate): Maintaining the Peace. The challenge of holding the vast and complex system together, fighting internal and external threats to its stability.
Generation 3 (Caligula alternate): The Cracks in the Peace. The story of how the peace becomes a gilded cage, and how the curse of its founders threatens to shatter it from within.


Terminology: Characters will refer to the known world as "the Pax." Enemies will be "breakers of the Peace." The historical period will be "the dawn of the Julian Peace." This language will constantly reinforce the central theme of the campaign.
The name is set. The world is The Pax Julia. We are now ready to build out the full, detailed campaign arcs on this powerful and evocative foundation.
Class: The Imperator (Pax Julia Edition)
Hit Die: 1d10 per level.
Primary Stats: Strength & Charisma.
Proficiencies: All Armor, Shields, Simple & Martial Weapons.
Saving Throws: Constitution, Charisma. (CON saves are vital for a General maintaining concentration on Haste or Spirit Guardians).
I. The Core Mechanics
Level 1: Auctoritas (Full Spellcasting)
Instead of "Divine Magic," you wield Auctoritas—the sheer weight of your command over reality.
Progression: You are a Full Caster (same spell slot progression as a Bard/Wizard). You gain access to 9th Level spells at Level 17.
Spell List: You select spells from the Imperator List (see below), which combines the best buffs of the Cleric, the defenses of the Wizard, and the Smites of the Paladin.
Spellcasting Focus: Your Signet Ring or a Weapon.
Level 1: Tactical Sense (Miasma)
You can smell the flow of battle.
Passive: You cannot be Surprised.
Active: As a Bonus Action, you can detect the location of any creature within 60 feet that is Invisible, Hidden, or Frightened. You know their exact hit point current vs. maximum ratio.
Level 2: Tyrant’s Decree (Psychic Smite)
This is your bread and butter. It scales slightly better than standard Smite because you have higher-level slots.
When you hit a creature with a melee weapon attack, you can expend a spell slot to deal Psychic Damage.
Damage: 2d8 for a 1st-level slot, plus 1d8 for each spell level higher than 1st, to a maximum of 6d8.
The "General" Buff: If the target is Frightened or Prone, add an additional 1d8 damage.
Level 2: Fighting Style
Choose Defense (+1 AC), Dueling (+2 Dmg), or Interception (Reduce damage to allies).
Level 3: Sacred Oath (Oath of the Eagle)
See Subclass below.
Level 5: Extra Attack
You can attack twice whenever you take the Attack action on your turn.
(Note: You get this 3 levels earlier than the Bardadin).
Level 6: Aura of Destiny (The Power Spike)
This is the ability the Bardadin sacrifices. You keep it.
Starting at 6th level, whenever you or a friendly creature within 10 feet of you makes a Saving Throw, they gain a bonus to the saving throw equal to your Charisma Modifier (minimum of +1).
Radii: At 18th level, the range of this aura increases to 30 feet.
Level 10: Aura of Courage
You and friendly creatures within 10 feet of you cannot be Frightened.
Level 11: Improved Tyrant’s Decree
Your strikes are so heavy they fracture the mind. Whenever you hit a creature with a melee weapon, the creature takes an extra 1d8 Psychic damage.
Level 14: War Caster’s Assault
The Action Economy Fix.
When you use your Action to cast a Spell of 1st Level or higher, you can make one Weapon Attack as a Bonus Action.
Why this beats Bardadin: The Bardadin has to choose between Casting Hold Person OR Attacking. You do both.
Level 16: The Sovereign's Word (Legendary Resistance)
The ability you requested.
You have bent reality to your will so many times that it refuses to break you.
Effect: You gain 3 Legendary Resistances per Long Rest. If you fail a Saving Throw, you can choose to succeed instead.
Buff: When you use this feature, you gain Temporary HP equal to your Paladin Level (16).
Level 20 Capstone: Apotheosis (Divus Gaia)
A better, more "General" Level 20.
As an Action, you ascend. For 1 minute:
Invulnerable Mind: You are Immune to Psychic Damage, Charmed, Stunned, and Paralyzed.
Infinite Reserves: You regenerate 10 Hit Points at the start of every turn.
The Voice of God: You can cast Command and Misty Step at will (no spell slot required) as a Legendary Action (you can take one Legendary Action at the end of another creature's turn).
Absolute Smite: Your Tyrant's Decree damage die becomes d12s instead of d8s.

II. Subclass: Oath of the Eagle
The specific mechanics for a Roman General.
Level 3: Channel Divinity
Kneel (Mass CC): As an Action, force enemies within 30ft to make a Wisdom Save. Fail = Prone and Speed 0.
Rally (Sustain): As a Bonus Action, you and allies within 30ft gain Temporary HP equal to your Charisma Score (e.g., +20 Temp HP).
Level 7: Aura of Conquest
Enemies within 10 feet of you have Disadvantage on Saving Throws against your spells.
Why this is 20% Stronger: A Bardadin casts Hypnotic Pattern with a DC of 17. You cast it with a DC of 17 AND the enemies roll with Disadvantage. Your spells stick.
Level 15: The Final Crossing
If you drop to 0 Hit Points, you drop to 1 Hit Point instead. When you do, you release a Psychic Shockwave. Enemies within 30 feet take 10d10 Psychic Damage. Once per Long Rest.

Cantrips:
Guidance (Tactics)

This is the magic of your enemies (The Vestals, Sulla’s Augurs).
Nature: This is Contract Law with the Gods. It is rigid, ritualistic, and slow. Do ut des ("I give so that you may give").
The Conflict: Sulla’s priests use magic to Bind and Preserve. You use magic to Change and Destroy. Your mere presence disrupts their rituals.
Mechanic: The Defiler. If you enter a Temple consecrated to the Old Laws (Jupiter/Saturn), your Miasma flares. You must make a Charisma Save or accidentally cast Thaumaturgy (tremors, dimming lights), revealing your nature.

VII. The Ecology of the Dying Republic: Factions & Powers
XIX. The Global Mechanic: The Game of Wolves
Philosophy: Rome is not a state; it is a crime scene waiting to happen. There are no "Good Guys." There are only Butchers (who hold power) and Cattle (who provide it). At age 16, you are a prize heifer that has realized she has horns.
Objective: You do not "join" these factions. You are the Singularity. You manipulate them, pit them against one another, and eventually swallow them whole to form the Empire.
The Reputation System: 
Your standing is measured on two axes.
Dignitas (Legitimacy): How much the "Old World" (Senate, Gods, Sulla) respects your bloodline.
High Dignitas: You are invited to balls, temples, and parleys.
Low Dignitas: You are hunted like a dog.


Infamia (Notoriety): How much the "New World" (Plebs, Criminals, Soldiers) fears your legend.
High Infamia: Gangs step aside; prices drop; mercenaries fight for free.
Low Infamia: You are seen as a victim to be robbed.

Motto: "Felix Roma" (Lucky Rome – Enforced by the Sword).
Archetype: The Stagnant Order / The Police State.

Sulla wants a garden of stone; you are the ivy cracking the marble.

Appearance: They wear blackened armor and crimson cloaks. They never speak. Sulla’s chirurgeons have removed their tongues and portions of their frontal lobes to ensure absolute obedience.
Mechanic (The Terminators): If your Infamia rises too high, a Red Squad (CR 5 Encounter) is dispatched. They do not accept bribes. They cannot be seduced (Immune to Charm). They track you tirelessly.
Key NPC: Lucius Cornelius Sulla (The Dictator)
Role: The Dying King / The Lich.
Appearance: His face is a ruin, ravaged by a flesh-eating disease (a side effect of resisting the Miasma). He smells of rot and expensive perfume. He rules from a darkened villa, surrounded by trembling scribes.
The Dynamic: He is the "Bad Father." He knows you are running. He sends you letters mocking your attempts to escape, testing your intelligence. He sees you as the only worthy opponent left, even if you are a child.

Assets to Plunder:
The Proscription Lists: The master list of every wealthy Roman marked for death. Stealing this allows you to blackmail the entire Senate.

Key NPC: Quintus Sertorius (The White Fawn)
Role: The King in Exile.
Location: Spain (Hispania).
Lore: The greatest general of the age (besides Sulla). He is holding out in Spain, waging a guerilla war. He has a pet white fawn that he claims speaks with the voice of Diana.
The Dynamic: He is your "Good Father" figure, but he is far away. Your goal in Act 2 is to reach him.
Assets to Plunder:
The Veteran Legions: Old soldiers hiding in the countryside who will only answer to the name "Caesar."

The Opportunity: The Shadow Army
You cannot fight Sulla’s legions in the field. But you can hire the Collegia to start riots, poison water supplies, and assassinate captains. They are your first "Legion." This is where Gaia learns the most important lesson of her life: Honor is a lie; Gold is truth.
Key NPC: Marcus Licinius Crassus (The Golden Vulture)
Role: The Shadow Investor.
Appearance: A man of average height with eyes like flint. He is not a gangster; he is a landlord. He owns the Collegia, though he pretends to be a respectable Senator.
The Dynamic: He is the first man to realize your potential. He doesn't want to kill you; he wants to buy shares in you. He funds your escape in exchange for a "future favor" (which will eventually be the Triumvirate).
Seduction Difficulty: High. He only gets aroused by profit margins.

Role: The Matriarch of Vice.

Role: The Oracle.

XXVI. Inter-Faction Dynamics (The Sandbox Mechanics)
The factions are not static; they form a complex web of hatred. You survive by playing them off one another.
1. The "Subura Riot" Strategy (Collegia vs. Optimates)
The Plan: You pay Mama Cama to start a food riot in the lower districts using stolen grain.
The Reaction: Sulla is forced to move his Red Centurions out of the Noble District to crush the riot.
The Opportunity: This leaves your family estate unguarded. You break in and steal the Sword of Mars.
2. The "Heretic" Strategy (Vestals vs. Optimates)
The Plan: You leak a rumor that Sulla is practicing necromancy (which he is) to sustain his life. You provide forged proof to the Vestals.
The Reaction: The Vestals threaten to extinguish the Sacred Flame if Sulla doesn't perform a cleansing rite.
The Opportunity: Sulla is paralyzed by a religious crisis for 1d4 days. The Heat on you drops.
3. The "Trojan Horse" Strategy (Pirates vs. Collegia)
The Plan: In Act 2, you return to Rome with the Pirate Fleet. You contact Crassus.
The Reaction: You offer Crassus a percentage of the loot if he lets your pirates smuggle into the city via Collegia tunnels.
The Opportunity: You now have 500 pirates inside the city walls for the Act 3 finale, bypassing the walls entirely.

XXVII. The "Heat" Meter (Survival Mechanics)
Since you are hunted by the State, you cannot roam freely. The DM tracks a Heat Level (1-10) based on your actions.
Heat 1-3 (The Ghost):
Environment: Safe.
Encounters: Thugs, Thieves, Drunks.
Status: You can walk the streets in a cloak. Shops sell to you.
Heat 4-7 (The Fugitive):
Environment: Hostile.
Encounters: City Watch, Bounty Hunters, Spies.
Status: Posters with your face are on walls. Guards actively search carriages. Shops require a Bribe or Intimidation check to serve you. You must use Disguise Self or the Mirror of Helen.
Heat 8-10 (The Public Enemy):
Environment: Deadly.
Encounters: Red Centurions, Vestal Battle-Mages.
Status: The gates are sealed. Anyone seen helping you is executed.
Constraint: You cannot Long Rest in the city unless you are in a Secure Safehouse (Collegia/Vestal).
Reducing Heat:
The Scapegoat: Frame someone else for your crimes. (Requires Deception check + Evidence planting).
The Bribe: Pay the Collegia to "clean" the streets of witnesses.
The Silence: Kill all witnesses to a specific event.

XXVIII. The "Seduction" Economy (Spending Your Charisma)
You don't just find gold in chests. You trade in Favors. You maintain a literal list of what people owe you.
The Favor Ledger:
Saved Cama’s nephew from a Sullan patrol. (Debt: One Assassination)
Stole a rare scroll for Servilia. (Debt: One Divination Ritual)
Drank the Pirate Bo'sun under the table. (Debt: Absolute Loyalty in Mutiny)
The "Gilded Chain" Mechanic:
You can use your Special Trait (Beauty/Charisma) to extract favors without doing work.
Action: You roll Persuasion against a target.
Success: They do the favor hoping for a reward (romance) later.
The Risk: If you never pay out, they become "Scorned Enemies" (Disadvantage on future social checks). You have to balance the tease with the reward. This is the core of the Hedonist Playstyle.

1. The Augurs of the Iron Wheel (Sulla’s Mages)
Sigil: A Blindfolded Eye inside a Gear.
Philosophy: Fate is Fixed. They use divination to ensure Sulla remains in power. They believe the "Republic" is a cosmic mandate.
The Threat: They can track you via Scrying.
Counter-Strategy: You must stay mobile. Or, you must kill the Augur Maximus and steal his Lituus (Wand), blinding Sulla’s spiritual surveillance.

Sigil: A Meteorite dripping blood.
Philosophy: Ecstatic Madness. Based in the Subura, these are the eunuch-priests of Cybele. They whip themselves in the streets. They are chaotic, loud, and terrifying.
The Opportunity: They hate the stuffy Roman pantheon. You can recruit them. They serve as your Bards and Berserkers. Their music drives the mobs into a frenzy during your riots.
The Risk: They are insane. If you don't feed them blood (enemies), they might turn on you.
3. The Sibylline Sisterhood (The Keepers of the Books)
Sigil: A Scroll burning at both ends.
Philosophy: The End is Coming. They guard the Sibylline Books, which contain the true history of the world (and the multiverse). They know about the Miasma.
The Interaction: They are neutral observers. They will trade you Scrolls (Spell knowledge) in exchange for you destroying abominations (creatures from other timelines that slipped through).

XXXII. Artifacts of the Bloodline (The Ancestral Loot)
You are not looting generic +1 swords. You are reclaiming the lost treasures of Troy and the Julian line.
1. The Mirror of Helen
Wondrous Item, Rare (Requires Attunement by a Woman)
Lore: A silver hand-mirror said to have belonged to Helen of Troy. It does not show your reflection; it shows your "Ideal Self."
Effect: While holding it, you can cast Disguise Self at will.
The Curse: If you stare into it for too long (1 hour+), you become Narcissistic (Disadvantage on Wisdom Checks) for the rest of the day, obsessed with your own perfection.
2. The cestus of Venus (The Girdle)
Wondrous Item, Very Rare
Lore: A belt woven from golden thread. It radiates a faint heat.
Effect: Your Charisma Score increases by 2. Additionally, you have Advantage on Persuasion checks against anyone who could be sexually attracted to you.
Active Ability: Once per day, you can use The Goddess’s Voice. You cast Mass Suggestion (DC 15) without a spell slot. The command must be "Love Me" or "Protect Me."
3. The Sword of Aeneas (The Trojan Blade)
Weapon (Gladius), Legendary (Act 3 Reward)
Lore: The bronze sword carried by your ancestor out of the burning ruins of Troy. It thirsts for the blood of Greeks and Traitors.
Effect: +2 Weapon. Deals extra 2d6 Necrotic Damage to anyone who has betrayed you or broken an oath.
Sentience: The sword has the personality of a weary, ruthless general. It whispers tactical advice in battle ("He is guarding his left flank; strike the right").

XXXIII. The "Ascension" Mechanic: Becoming a Goddess
In D&D terms, you are gaining levels. In Narrative terms, you are undergoing Apotheosis.
Stage 1: The Mortal (Levels 1-4)
You are flesh and blood. You bleed red.
The Miasma hurts you (Migraines, Nosebleeds).
Visual: You look like a very beautiful human.
Stage 2: The Demigod (Levels 5-10)
You stop getting sick. You stop feeling cold.
The Miasma integrates. You bleed Ichor (Gold-flecked blood).
Visual: The "Uncanny Valley" effect begins. Your skin is too perfect. Your eyes glow in the dark. Animals are afraid of you.
Stage 3: The Goddess (Levels 11-20)
You are an entity. You stop aging entirely.
The Miasma obeys you.
Visual: You affect the environment. Flowers bloom when you walk past. Shadows reach out to touch you. When you are angry, the sky darkens (Thumaturgy effect is permanent). You are Divus Iulius.

XXVII. The "Heat" Meter (Survival Mechanics)
Since you are hunted by the State, you cannot roam freely. The DM tracks a Heat Level (1-10) based on your actions.
Heat 1-3 (The Ghost):
Environment: Safe.
Encounters: Thugs, Thieves, Drunks.
Status: You can walk the streets in a cloak. Shops sell to you.
Heat 4-7 (The Fugitive):
Environment: Hostile.
Encounters: City Watch, Bounty Hunters, Spies.
Status: Posters with your face are on walls. Guards actively search carriages. Shops require a Bribe or Intimidation check to serve you. You must use Disguise Self or the Mirror of Helen.
Heat 8-10 (The Public Enemy):
Environment: Deadly.
Encounters: Red Centurions, Vestal Battle-Mages.
Status: The gates are sealed. Anyone seen helping you is executed.
Constraint: You cannot Long Rest in the city unless you are in a Secure Safehouse (Collegia/Vestal).
Reducing Heat:
The Scapegoat: Frame someone else for your crimes. (Requires Deception check + Evidence planting).
The Bribe: Pay the Collegia to "clean" the streets of witnesses.
The Silence: Kill all witnesses to a specific event.
