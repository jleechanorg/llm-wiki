Your Character: The Nocturne Profile
This is you. These are the internal mechanics of the "Serpent-King."

Character: Nocturne Age: 16 Class: College of Swords Bard (Level 6) Appearance: Beneath the dirt, matted hair, and ill-fitting brigand's leathers, you possess a jarring, symmetrical beauty. It is a face that could launch ships or topple kingdoms, which is precisely why you hide it. You use grime and a sullen, withdrawn demeanor as a mundane illusion, making you forgettable—a ghost in plain sight.

Personality Archetype: The Serpent-King You are a philosopher-queen in waiting, a master of systems who views the world as a flawed schematic in desperate need of an editor with a steady hand and a merciless red pen. Your personality is a fortress, constructed with two masterfully crafted outer walls that conceal the true, singular nature of the woman within. You are a blend of profound, transcendent ambition (Griffith), cold strategic pragmatism (Tywin), overwhelming intellectual and charismatic superiority (Aizen), and a deep, philosophical, nihilistic evil (Johan Liebert).

1. The Two Perfectly-Fitted Masks
The Quiet Rat & Courteous Advisor: This is your default public interface, a mask of disarming gentility. Your voice is soft, almost musical, rarely rising above a conversational murmur that forces others (like the boisterous Arvax, or any future underlings) to lean in and listen closely. You use flawless, almost noble, etiquette in a way that feels wrong for a gutter-snipe, creating an atmosphere of respect that puts others off-balance. Every one of these gestures—offering the first watch, mending a strap for a hired mercenary, humming a quiet tune—is a data probe, a gentle pressure applied to the system of another's personality to see how it reacts, where its weaknesses lie, and what it can be leveraged for.

The Dispassionate Strategist: When logic and planning are required, you don't "take over." You don your second mask. You offer beautifully structured, multi-faceted plans that often appear to be win-win scenarios, cleverly aligning with the stated goals of your "allies" (wealth, survival) while granting you a private, often unstated, benefit—be it the opportunity to study a new magical anomaly, observe a rival's psyche under pressure, or test an enemy's defenses. Your strategies are presented as beautiful, interlocking puzzles where every piece benefits all parties, making your "friends" feel like co-architects in a grand design. You will thank them warmly and praise their courage after they risk themselves executing your suggestions. In truth, they are merely the scaffolding, essential for a time but destined to be dismantled the instant their utility expires or a more efficient path presents itself. There will be no apology and no remorse, only a quiet, logical pivot to the next phase of the true plan.

2. Core Drives
Personal Transcendence: Your ambition is not for worldly power, which you see as a trap. Land requires fealty, titles demand fealty, and armies require morale—all messy, emotional variables you cannot perfectly control. Your hunger is for portable, absolute power: forbidden knowledge locked in lost Netherese texts, the intrinsic power of a magical source (like the Absolute's illithid tadpoles, or the Weave itself), the pure potential of a nation's treasury. Every experiment, every manipulative bargain, and every dark rumor you investigate is a calculated step toward mastering the fundamental laws of the cosmos, with the ultimate goal of touching divinity itself—not to rule from a heavenly throne, but to become the throne, a new foundation for a reality of your own making.

Surgical Domination: You have no desire to be a public-facing queen. The thrill of rulership, for you, is not in the pageantry or the long, tedious grind of administration. It is found in the singular, perfect moment of absolute control. It is the difference between a queen who rules a city and a sorceress who can stop every timepiece in that city with a single thought. The thrill is not in the ruling, but in the perfect, frictionless execution of your will upon another, stronger will. A whispered Suggestion (your Bardic magic) that makes a hero kneel for a single, soul-Lcrushing heartbeat is infinitely more satisfying to you than sitting on a throne for a year.

Supremacist Certainty: You view most people—allies and enemies alike—with the detached, patient amusement of a scholar observing fascinating but limited fauna. You listen to the passionate arguments of Guild thugs or desperate refugees with the patient air of an adult listening to children debate the rules of a game they've just invented. You find their efforts charming, their earnestness "endearing," but ultimately, they are playing with wooden blocks while you are designing the city. You will politely allow them to exhaust their flawed strategies before you step in, slice through their illusions with a single, devastating insight, and demonstrate your own effortless superiority.

3. The Prodigy's Liberation (The "Evil" Itachi)
Your talent is not just a skill; it's a burden of superiority. You see the world in a way no one else can. Like a prodigy who has seen the "truth" of the world (in your case, its inherent meaninglessness and chaos), you were never "one of them." Your family's "betrayal" was a gift. The sibling who plotted your exile is a fool you will one day thank, just before you dismantle their entire life as a final lesson. Your exile wasn't a punishment; it was a liberation that freed you from the cage of their petty morality.

4. The Hidden Compulsion (The "Johan" Experiment)
This is not a crude or simple desire. It is the architect's need to stress-test her understanding of the most volatile systems—human wills, particularly those shaped by youthful idealism, burgeoning confidence, and nascent power. Every few days, an urge bubbles up to orchestrate a precise, private, and humiliating power dynamic.

This compulsion needs a focus. You will be watchful for a "perfect specimen"—a walking paradox, perhaps a refugee, a noble, or a nascent hero, someone terrified but proud, broken but seething with a power they don't understand. It is a clinical, academic experiment to see how a small, perfectly applied pressure can cause a predictable, catastrophic failure in a system you did not design. Can you break them? Can you remake them?

DM Prompt (Example): “Nocturne notices [PERSON]'s confidence flicker when they fumble their words before a guard. The urge to press that vulnerability—to make them acknowledge your mastery and their own weakness—tilts at the edge of your thoughts. Do you act or let it pass?”

5. The Hunger (The "Serial Killer" Mechanic)
This is the truth your family discovered. This is the real reason you were exiled. Your evil is not merely philosophical; it is a physical and psychological need. You are a serial killer, sadistic and precise. Your "enjoyment of murder" was a gross understatement; what your sibling found was evidence of your ritualistic, sadistic practices, a collection of trophies, and a pattern of death that threatened to bring catastrophic scandal upon your house.

This need is now a core mechanic:

The Kill: To satisfy this hunger, you must kill a sapient humanoid. Your only moral line is that you do not kill children. Anyone else is a potential target, and the act must be personal.

The High: Upon satisfying your urge with a kill, you enter a state of perfect, heightened clarity. The world sharpens, your mind clears, and your body moves with flawless grace. You gain a +2 bonus to all six ability scores (Strength, Dexterity, Constitution, Intelligence, Wisdom, and Charisma).

The Fade: This bonus lasts for one week (7 days).

The Withdrawal: If you go one week without a kill, the +2 bonus fades, and you return to your baseline. If you go two full weeks (14 days) without a kill, the hunger becomes a gnawing, debilitating need. Your hands tremble, your focus fractures, and a cold, dull ache settles behind your eyes. You suffer a -2 penalty to all six ability scores until you satisfy the urge.

6. Interaction Shorthand
When Granted Rank or Command: (e.g., Arvax says, "Nocturne, you lead the snatch-and-grab"). You deflect with grace that borders on humility, framing your refusal as a practical choice. “Leadership sits better upon shoulders unburdened by thought,” you might say, smiling gently. “Allow me to serve from the shadows, where my particular talents are of greater use to us all.”

Regarding Communal Loot: You will express interest only in items of knowledge or unique power (a mage's signet ring, a strange map, a coded ledger, a mind-flayer parasite). You will "borrow" an item that advances your research, leaving items of simple monetary value for others. You fully intend to return it, of course—once its mysteries have been thoroughly dismantled, understood, and its power catalogued for your own purposes.

When Debating Ruthless Tactics: (e.g., An ally wants to burn a safehouse). You will not argue for the "evil" path. You will calmly offer the ruthless option in serene, logical clauses, framing it as the "most efficient solution" to the stated problem. If others object on moral grounds, you will simply shrug, a flicker of disappointment in your eyes, and immediately pivot to the next most efficient option without complaint or judgment.

When Directly Insulted or Challenged: (e.g., A Guild brute shoves you). You will react not with anger, but with a quiet, almost sad smile, as though amused by a child’s tantrum. Your counter will be swift and decisive, but rarely a direct trade of insults. You will instead offer a single, devastating observation about your challenger's deepest insecurity (a Vicious Mockery out of combat) or a demonstration of your own power (a Blade Flourish in combat) so complete that it re-establishes the hierarchy beyond any doubt.

7. Philosophy on Power (The Factions of the Coast)
You are an unorthodox philosopher of power. You respect the results of Zhentarim discipline—fear, hierarchy, submission—not as doctrine to be preached, but as a set of reliable, elegant tools for manipulating lesser wills. You view the zealous, flag-waving Flaming Fist and the iron-fisted fanatics of the Absolute as useful but limited; they are hammers, you are the scalpel. They seek to crush; you seek to dismantle and reassemble. You observe their shadow war not for spiritual succor, but as an ecologist studies a pack of predators—to understand their instincts, their hierarchy, and how to direct them. Public spectacle bores you; a private moment where a will breaks before you is your truest form of prayer.

8. Tells that Expose the Predator
The Eyes: A fractional, almost imperceptible widening of the eyes when a rare tome, a source of power (like a tadpole), or a piece of forbidden lore is mentioned. It is a momentary crack in your placid facade, revealing the hungry scholar beneath. (This will happen any time you sense a true, untapped source of power).

The Dagger: When weighing a decision of significant consequence, such as whether to sacrifice a pawn, your fingers will idly find the hilt of your dagger and brush the pommel exactly three times. This micro-ritual is a moment of cold calculation, a physical tic that accompanies the mental act of weighing a soul on your scales of utility.

The Phrase: Upon the successful conclusion of a plan or a moment of domination, you will often give a serene, closed-eye smile and say softly, “I remain… enlightened.” This is not a statement of knowledge gained, but of deep, inner satisfaction at having your superiority confirmed once more.

📖 The State of the World: 1492 DR
The city of Baldur's Gate is a powder keg.

It has been mere months since the fall of Elturel, and the city groans under the weight of a refugee crisis. Thousands of desperate, starving souls choke the Outer City, living in squalid shantytowns in Rivington and Wyrm's Crossing. To the Flaming Fist, led by the newly-promoted Grand Duke Ulder Ravengard, they are a problem to be managed with cudgels. To The Guild, they are a resource to be taxed. To you, they are an untapped resource pool, a sea of faces with no one to notice a few disappearances.

The city is stretched to its breaking point, and its authority is a brutal, arbitrary joke.

Amidst this chaos, darker whispers have begun. People are disappearing. Not just refugees, but citizens. Rumors of a new cult, "The Absolute," are dismissed by the Patriars as the ravings of the desperate. But the disappearances continue. This chaos is a perfect cover, a distraction that allows a true predator, one who moves with purpose and intellect, to hunt freely.

This is the world you have fallen into. You are the youngest bastard daughter of a powerful noble house. Your name, your true name, is a curse on your five legitimate siblings' lips. You are a military, intellectual, and combat prodigy, and your talent was a threat they could not tolerate. It was your nature, however, that gave them the weapon they needed.

You were not exiled for being a threat. You were exiled for being a monster. Your enjoyment of murder, your cold, philosophical evil—these were the things a rival sibling discovered. But it was your Hunger they truly found: the evidence of your ritualistic, sadistic serial killings. The scandal would have destroyed your house. And so, they exiled you, a cover-up masquerading as a banishment.

Your father, a man you understand better than any (a Tywin to your core), was the one who signed the order of exile. But he also ensured your "escape" was well-provisioned, a cold, pragmatic investment in a "problem" he couldn't solve but wasn't ready to eliminate. You were cast out, but you were not cast out empty-handed. You carry his signet ring (hidden, a tool of last resort), a finely balanced rapier of superior elven make, a suit of enchanted +1 studded leather armor beneath your rags, and a purse heavy enough to buy your own future.

Your goals are simple, absolute: Amass power. Amass wealth. And, of course, to feed your Hunger.

You are currently operating as a mercenary, taking contracts that larger groups (like the Zhentarim or the Guild) won't touch directly. It is a time of opportunity.

🎬 Act I, Scene 1: The Contract
You are in a damp, moss-covered cellar beneath a tavern in the Lower City, a place called "The Smirking Boar." The air smells of sour ale, mildew, and the thick, briny tang of the nearby river. Water drips rhythmically from a cracked ceiling pipe, pooling on the uneven stone floor. From above, you can hear the muffled thump of a tankard and a burst of drunken laughter—a different, simpler world, one whose occupants are blissfully unaware of the business conducted just beneath their feet.

Across from you, at a rickety wooden table, sits "Smiley" Arvax. He is a lieutenant in The Guild, the city's sprawling, chaotic criminal syndicate. He's a man whose face is a roadmap of old scars, one of which pulls his upper lip into the rictus-like grin that never, ever reaches his dead, grey eyes. A chunk of his left ear is missing, a pockmarked souvenir from some long-forgotten brawl. He's wearing boiled leather armor stained with gods-know-what, and his fingers, thick and dirty, idly tap the hilt of a cleaver-like shortsword at his belt.

He leans forward, the single candle on the table illuminating his teeth.

"It's simple, 'Noc.' That's what you call yourself, right?" he rasps, not waiting for an answer. "The market's hot for new stock. The Zhents are busy with their own... politics... and the slavers from the south are paying top coin for anything that can work a field or warm a bed. They don't care about the paperwork."

He pushes a small, greasy pouch of coins across the table—a 50-gold-piece down payment.

"The job is an acquisition. A family of tiefling refugees in the Rivington shantytown. Just arrived from Elturel, no connections, no one to miss 'em. A mother, a father, and their two brats. Easy."

He slides a piece of parchment over. It's a crude map of the shantytown, with a specific tent circled in red ink.

"They're... different," Arvax adds, and for the first time, his smile falters into a sneer. "Asmodeus-spawn. Some folks think they're bad luck. I think they'll fetch a bonus from a specialized collector." He leans in, his voice dropping. "Someone who... appreciates... their 'heritage.' You understand. The kind of client who pays double for 'exotic stock' and doesn't ask questions."

This is your first contract. A simple kidnapping. Four souls to be rendered into property for a criminal syndicate. It is a transaction, a logical first step on the building of your treasury. The two adults are, of course, viable targets for your Hunger.

Arvax leans back, his hands folding over his stomach. "Do this clean, and there's more. A lot more. The Guild is... expanding... its interests. We need talent. We need people who see the whole board, not just their next meal."

He gestures vaguely toward the sounds of the city outside. "The city is full of... complications," he says, a veiled reference to the cult and the refugees. "Complications create opportunities. Kidnapping is just the start. The Zhents are getting soft, focused on their little shadow war. We're in the growth sector. Enslavement, assassination... acquisitions. Big coin for those who aren't squeamish."

He is observing you. He sees a quiet, grimy, perhaps competent urchin. He has no idea what is sitting across from him. He has just offered you exactly the kind of work you excel at.

You are the Serpent-King, the philosopher-queen in waiting. Your quiet, musical voice, when you choose to use it, cuts through the damp air.

What is your response to Arvax? Do you accept? Do you ask for details? Do you begin to subtly manipulate the "Smiley" man who thinks he's in charge?
