There wasn’t a single fixed number, as the Republic raised legions reactively for campaigns, and legions were often understrength due to casualties, detachments, recruitment issues, or disease.
Legion Size
A standard Roman legion in the late Republic (post-Marian reforms, which Caesar’s army followed) had a paper strength of about 4,800–5,200 infantry (organized into 10 cohorts), plus a small cavalry contingent (~120–300 men) and support staff. In practice, Caesar’s legions were frequently closer to 3,000–4,500 men each due to operational realities.
Total Roman Army (Republic-Wide)
At the time of Caesar’s assassination in 44 BC, ancient sources and modern estimates indicate there were around 37 legions in existence across the Roman world (including some in the East like Macedonia, Spain, and other provinces). This would suggest a theoretical total of roughly 150,000–200,000 legionaries (excluding auxiliaries, allies, or naval forces), though many were understrength or dispersed in garrisons.
The overall Republican army (including Italian allies and auxiliaries) could swell much higher during major mobilizations, but peacetime or average standing strength was lower.
Forces Under Julius Caesar’s Direct Command
This is often what people mean when asking about “Rome’s troops at the time of Julius Caesar,” especially regarding his famous campaigns:
	•	During the Gallic Wars (peak around 52–50 BC), Caesar typically commanded 8–12 legions (most famously 10 at times), equating to roughly 40,000–60,000 legionaries, plus allied troops, auxiliaries, and cavalry (often 10,000+ additional non-citizen forces). His total effective field army in Gaul sometimes approached 70,000–80,000 men.
	•	In the Civil War (e.g., at Pharsalus in 48 BC), Caesar fielded about 8 legions with around 22,000 legionary infantry (plus cavalry and auxiliaries), significantly outnumbered by Pompey’s forces but victorious due to quality and tactics.
In short: The entire Roman state’s military manpower during Caesar’s era hovered in the low to mid hundreds of thousands when fully mobilized (legions + allies/auxiliaries), but active field armies were much smaller. Caesar himself never commanded more than about 10–12 legions (~50,000–70,000 men including supports) at any one time.
For deeper reading, sources like Caesar’s own Commentarii de Bello Gallico, Appian, Plutarch, and modern historians (e.g., Adrian Goldsworthy’s works on the Roman army) provide the basis for these estimates.
