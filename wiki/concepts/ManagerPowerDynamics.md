# Manager Power Dynamics

## Definition

Every interaction between a manager and a report carries implicit authority weight. Even casual technical engagement ("this could be better") reads as a check-in from a manager, because of the formal power relationship. This is especially dangerous for new managers who previously interacted with teammates as peers.

## Key Insight

Jeff Lee-Chan ([[JeffreyChan]]) identifies this as his biggest early management mistake: being so technically engaged that every comment implicitly felt like micromanagement to reports. The content of his observations was usually valuable, but the emotional impact was net negative because he wasn't weighing the authority asymmetry.

## Manifestations

- **Implicit check-in**: Asking "how's this database choice?" sounds like scrutiny from a manager, not curiosity from a peer
- **Text amplification**: Written comments (Slack, Google Docs) sound more ominous than the same words spoken in person
- **COVID effect**: Remote-first interaction (no in-person warmth) made the dynamic worse
- **Engagement ≠ support**: Being very involved and helpful can paradoxically demoralize reports

## Mitigation

- Preface check-ins: "Just checking in, nothing's wrong — I want to learn about this"
- Be aware that your words carry more weight than you intend
- Deliberately create space for reports to approach you, not just the reverse
- Especially important for IC→manager transitions where the person is still thinking in peer-mode
