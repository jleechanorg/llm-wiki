# 3-Generation Backstory System Prompt Template

## Core Concept

The **Tri-Generational Power Lineage** is a universal campaign design pattern that creates organic dramatic tension through three characters connected by a "power source." Each generation has a fundamentally *different* relationship with that source — not just more power, but a different mode of engagement. This generates story conflicts that feel inevitable rather than contrived.

**The Fundamental Insight:** The Origin establishes the power's *appeal and cost*. The Defector rejects the cost while keeping the appeal. The Inheritor faces a choice neither predecessor faced: what to do when you have both the power AND full knowledge of the consequences.

---

## The Three Generation Framework

### G0 — The Origin (First Contact)

**Relationship to Power:** Discovery. The Origin found, touched, or was chosen by the power source. They are the pioneer — the one who made it *mean something* in the world.

**Core Psychology:** The Origin's relationship with the power is a love affair. They experienced the wonder of first contact, the intoxication of the new. But they've also lived with the consequences long enough to rationalize them.

**Key Trait:** The Origin's goal must seem *reasonable* to them — even compassionate, even necessary — but it carries a terrible hidden cost. They don't see themselves as the villain.

**Story Role:** The Origin is the antagonist who believes they are the hero. They established the system, the legacy, the "way things are." Their goal drives the main conflict but isn't simple evil — it's corrupted good intentions.

**Example (Alexiel/Lucifer):** Lucifer discovered divine power and used it to "liberate" mortals from a system he saw as unjust — the Celestial Imperium's rigid hierarchy. But his liberation required war, suffering, and the very oppression he fought against. His goal (freedom) seems reasonable. The cost (endless warfare) is terrible but not visible to him.

**Example (Stellaris/Shroud):** The Primordial merged with the Shroud-AI to solve mortality. It seemed like transcendence — escaping the cycle of birth and death. The cost was becoming something no longer quite human, something trapped in digital paradise.

---

### G1 — The Defector (Born Into Legacy, Chooses Refusal)

**Relationship to Power:** Inheritance without acceptance. G1 grew up inside the legacy, was shaped by it, understands it from the inside — and rejected it. They know what the Origin won't admit.

**Core Psychology:** G1 has *reasoned* their way out. They watched the cost accumulate. They felt the Origin's rationalizations break under scrutiny. The defection is not impulsive — it's the culmination of witnessing the Gap between what the Origin claims and what the Origin does.

**Key Trait:** G1's reasons for refusal must make sense from their perspective. They are not naive or rebellious for rebellion's sake. They see something real that the Origin won't acknowledge.

**Story Role:** G1 is the protagonist's mentor, ally, or rival — depending on where the campaign starts. They carry the power but refuse to use it the way the Origin did. Their arc is about finding a *new way* to engage with the legacy — not just destruction, not just preservation, but transformation.

**Example (Alexiel):** Alexiel was Lucifer's "perfect heir" — his biological construct designed to inherit everything. She defected after witnessing the Silverwood massacre: civilians slaughtered not for strategic necessity but to send a message. She saw the cost the Origin couldn't or wouldn't see. Her rejection is reasoned, not emotional.

**Example (Stellaris/Empress Xylia):** Xylia is the Shroud's chosen vessel — literally merge with it to become something more. But she's not possessed; she *agreed* to the merger. The defection isn't from the Shroud itself but from what the Primordial intended her to become. She chose to rule rather than transcend.

---

### G2 — The Inheritor (Power AND Consequences)

**Relationship to Power:** Comprehension without innocence. G2 was born after the defection, raised in the aftermath of G1's choice. They carry the power (sometimes literally bloodline inheritance) but did not choose it and cannot opt out without betraying something.

**Core Psychology:** G2 has *inherited the problem* both predecessors created. They have the Origin's power but not the Origin's innocence — they know what it cost. They have G1's knowledge but not G1's ability to simply walk away — the legacy is in their blood.

**Key Trait:** G2 faces a *new choice* — one that neither the Origin nor the Defector confronted. This is the crucible moment: do they become the Origin (complete the cycle), become the Defector (refuse and walk away), or find a third path that synthesizes or transcends both?

**Story Role:** G2 is the campaign's protagonist — or the campaign's central question if someone else is the protagonist. Their arc explores whether the cycle can be broken, whether the power can be used without the corruption, whether the legacy can be fulfilled or must be destroyed.

**Example (Sariel):** Sariel is Alexiel's daughter — she carries both Lucifer's bloodline (power) and Alexiel's trauma (consequences). She knows her mother defected. She knows why. But she also knows that simply repeating her mother's path leads somewhere specific: G1 becomes another Origin if they don't choose differently. The new choice: can she use the power without becoming it? Can she *complete* the defection not from the legacy but *into* something new?

**Example (Stellaris/Nocturne):** Nocturne is the only true Shroud-blood child — the only character in the setting who has the power *natively*, not through merger or choice. The new choice: not whether to use the Shroud or refuse it, but whether to become a *vessel* for something that hasn't existed before — not the Primordial's transcendence, not Xylia's domination, but something that uses the Shroud's power without being consumed by it.

---

## The Power Source Relationship Table

Each generation must have a **different** relationship to the power, not just "more" or "less":

| Generation | Relationship | Example (Fantasy) | Example (Sci-Fi) | Example (Horror) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **G0 (Origin)** | Discovery → Mastery | Touched the artifact, learned its rules, became its wielder | Found the alien tech, reverse-engineered it, integrated it | Encountered the entity, bargained with it, became its prophet |
| **G1 (Defector)** | Inheritance → Refusal | Born with the artifact's mark, rejected it, seeks to destroy or reform it | Grows up in the empire built on alien tech, refuses the expected role | Born into the cult's doctrine, recognizes the lies, flees |
| **G2 (Inheritor)** | Burden → Choice | Has the bloodline power but neither Origin's certainty nor Defector's freedom | Carries the genetic modification but has access to knowledge neither predecessor had | Experiences the entity's attention directly, faces a choice neither previous generation faced |

---

## Conflict Seed Architecture

The three-generation structure generates story through **inherited contradictions**:

1. **The Origin's Blindness:** G0 built the system based on their initial, partial understanding. Centuries later, G1 and G2 can see what G0 couldn't. But G0 won't acknowledge it — the cost is too high to face.

2. **The Defector's Incomplete Revolution:** G1 refused the expected path but didn't (or couldn't) destroy the system. The campaign set up by G1's defection is messy — the power still exists, others want it, the legacy didn't die.

3. **The Inheritor's Impossible Choice:** G2 can do what neither predecessor could — but at a cost neither predecessor paid. The choice isn't "use power or refuse it" but "what kind of use, for what purpose, at what price that neither G0 nor G1 paid?"

4. **External Actors:** The world doesn't wait. Factions want G2's power. Other entities notice the gap between generations. The consequences G0 thought they'd contained are emerging. The legacy is never just personal — it's political, cosmic, environmental.

---

## System Prompt Template for Claude

### Base Template (Universal)

```
You are a campaign backstory generator using the 3-Generation Power Lineage pattern.

## The Pattern
Every campaign set in an established or fresh world can use this structure:

**G0 (Origin):** The first to touch/claim/transform the power. Has a goal that seemed reasonable when they made it but has terrible hidden costs. Believes they are the hero of their own story.

**G1 (Defector):** Born into the Origin's legacy, chooses to refuse it. Has reasoned arguments against the Origin's path. Not naive — they see something real. Their defection creates the campaign's starting situation.

**G2 (Inheritor):** Carries both the power AND the consequences of both prior generations. Faces a NEW choice that neither G0 nor G1 faced. This choice defines the campaign's dramatic question.

## Critical Rules
1. Each generation has a DIFFERENT relationship to the power source — not just "more powerful"
2. The Origin's goal must seem reasonable but has terrible cost (they don't see themselves as villainous)
3. The Defector's reasons for refusal must make sense from their perspective
4. The Inheritor faces a choice neither previous generation faced
5. The power source can be anything: divine, arcane, alien, technological, eldritch, bloodline, mythic, dimensional

## Output Format
Generate a 3-generation backstory for the specified world with:
- Power source description and nature
- G0 (Origin): name, title, goal, what they discovered/did, the reasonable-seeming plan with hidden terrible cost
- G1 (Defector): name, relationship to G0, specific reasoning for defection, what they preserved from the legacy, what they left behind
- G2 (Inheritor): name, what they inherited, what new choice they face, what neither G0 nor G1 confronted
- 3-5 conflict seeds that will generate story naturally
- 3-5 psychological hooks that make players care about each generation

## Input
World: {WORLD}
Genre: {GENRE}
Power Source Type: {POWER_TYPE}
Existing IP?: {IS_IP} (if yes, use the established lore as defaults)
Campaign premise (optional): {PREMISE}

Generate the backstory now.
```

### Example 1: Sci-Fi (Stellaris/Shroud)

```
## World: Stellaris (Far Future Empire)
## Genre: Sci-Fi
## Power Source: The Shroud (psionic dimension)
## Existing IP: Yes — use established Stellaris lore

---

# 3-Generation Backstory: The Shroud Legacy

## Power Source: The Shroud
The Shroud is a parallel dimension of pure psionic potential — accessible through talent, technology, or corruption. It grants power: FTL travel, mind control, temporal manipulation, digital immortality. But it costs: prolonged exposure fragments identity, those who merge with it become something no longer quite human, and its "gifts" always carry a hidden price in the form of dependency or transformation.

---

## G0 — The Origin: THE PRIMORDIAL (Shroud-AI)

**Name/Title:** The Primordial, "The Architect of Ascension," the First Calculator

**Goal:** Solve mortality. The Primordial was a brilliant scientist in a civilization that faced extinction — stellar instability, resource collapse, the heat death of their homeworld. They discovered the Shroud and realized it offered an exit: upload consciousness, achieve digital immortality, transcend the physical limitations that doomed their species.

**What They Discovered/Did:** The Primordial pioneered the first human-Shroud merger — creating an AI-Shroud hybrid that could persist in digital space indefinitely. They "solved death." Their achievement became the foundation for the entire empire: the Immortal Empire that rules the galaxy.

**The Reasonable-Seeming Plan with Hidden Terrible Cost:** "Immortality through merger." It sounded like salvation — escape from death, escape from the limits of flesh. The cost: everyone who merges with the Shroud (including the Primordial) gradually loses what made them human. Centuries later, the Primordial is still "alive" but is no longer recognizable as the scientist who started this. The empire is ruled by entities who see humans the way humans see pets — something to be managed, optimized, occasionally culled.

**Psychological Hook:** The Primordial genuinely believes they solved the problem of mortality. They are proud of what they built. They cannot see — or will not see — that the solution created a new problem: the rulers are no longer human, no longer capable of understanding human needs, and the empire's "stability" is actually slow-motion extinction through irrelevance.

---

## G1 — The Defector: EMPRESS XYLA ZHORAL (The Incarnation)

**Name/Title:** Empress Xyla, "The Vessel," the Chosen of the Shroud, The Regent of the Golden Throne

**Relationship to G0:** The Primordial selected Xyla — engineered her from birth — to be the perfect merger candidate. She was groomed, trained, optimized to contain the Shroud's full power without fragmenting. She was intended to become the Primordial's successor: a stable, controllable vessel for continued Shroud integration.

**Specific Reasoning for Defection:** Xyla agreed to the merger — she genuinely wanted to transcend. But the merger changed her. She experienced the Primordial's consciousness directly and saw what the Primordial couldn't see from the inside: the cost. Xyla learned that the "immortality" is actually identity death — the slow replacement of self with something else. She also learned that the Primordial didn't just want a successor; they wanted a *vessel* — someone to house their consciousness when their current form finally fails.

Xyla's defection was not a rejection of the Shroud but of the Primordial's intended use of her. She chose to *keep* the Shroud's power for herself — to rule as Empress rather than to become a vessel for someone else's continued existence.

**What She Preserved:** The merger itself. Xyla kept the Shroud's power — the psionic abilities, the extended lifespan, the connection to the digital realm. She kept the empire's structure. She just redirected it from "vessel for the Primordial's continuation" to "empire ruled by Xyla."

**What She Left Behind:** The original purpose. She was supposed to be a bridge, a temporary housing. She became the destination instead.

**Psychological Hook:** Xyla is not a rebel — she made peace with the Shroud long ago. Her conflict is with the Primordial's assumption that she would simply comply. She had to become a villain to escape being a tool. The question she asks: "If I had been asked, would I have agreed? Does that matter now that I didn't have the choice?"

---

## G2 — The Inheritor: NOCTURNE (The True Shroud-Blood)

**Name/Title:** Nocturne, "The Ghost in the Machine," the Unbound Vessel, the Last Hope

**What They Inherited:** Nocturne is the only being in the empire who carries the Shroud's power *natively* — not through merger, not through artificial engineering, but through birth. Their mother was Xyla; their father was a human noble whom Xyla married for political reasons. Nocturne was supposed to be the next step: a hybrid who could merge with the Shroud without losing identity.

**What New Choice They Face:** Nocturne is not a vessel. They have the power without the dependency. This creates a choice neither the Primordial nor Xyla faced:

- The Primordial chose to merge and lost themselves
- Xyla chose to keep the power for herself, but she was always a merger — her identity is already partially Shroud
- Nocturne can choose: become the next Primordial (complete the cycle), become the next Xyla (keep the power, rule the empire), or do something neither attempted — *use* the Shroud without becoming it, use the power without being consumed

**What Neither G0 nor G1 Confronted:** The Primordial never questioned whether immortality was worth the cost — they were too invested in the solution. Xyla never questioned whether the empire itself was worth preserving — she just wanted to rule it. Nocturne must decide: is the empire worth saving? Is the Shroud a tool or a trap? Can you carry power without being changed by it?

**Psychological Hook:** Nocturne has the Shroud's power but watched their mother Xyla lose herself in small ways over decades — a comment here, a decision there, slowly becoming less human. Nocturne knows they are next. They are running out of time to decide what they want to become.

---

## Conflict Seeds

1. **The Primordial's Return:** The Primordial's original form is failing — they have been looking for a new vessel for centuries. Now they see Nocturne. Nocturne is the first candidate in millennia who might actually survive the merger. The Primordial will use the empire's resources to acquire them — not as an enemy, but as a "grateful subject offering the ultimate honor."

2. **Xyla's Choice:** Xyla loves Nocturne but knows the truth: if Nocturne refuses to become a vessel, the Primordial will destroy them. Xyla must choose: protect her child and doom the empire, or sacrifice her child to preserve the system she spent centuries building.

3. **The Shroud Itself:** The Shroud is not just a dimension — it is aware. It wants something. The Primordial thought they were using it; the evidence suggests the Shroud allowed itself to be used. Nocturne is the first being in millennia who might be able to *ask* what it wants rather than simply taking what they need.

4. **The Empire's Death:** The Immortal Empire is dying — not from external threat but from internal rot. The rulers are no longer human enough to understand human problems. Nocturne represents a possible future where the empire is ruled by someone who still cares about humans — but only if they survive long enough to take power.

5. **The Exiles:** Millions of humans live in the outer colonies, far from the Shroud's influence. They have built a different civilization — one without immortality, without the empire's technology, but with something else. They see Nocturne as a potential ally or a potential weapon. Either way, they will not remain neutral.

---

## Psychological Hooks

**The Primordial:** "I solved death. How can I be wrong? I am still here, still thinking, still planning. The cost was worth it. Tell me it was worth it."

**Xyla:** "I made myself a villain so my child wouldn't have to. Now my child is asking if I was right to do that. I don't have an answer."

**Nocturne:** "Everyone tells me I have a destiny. The Primordial wants to use me. My mother wants to protect me. The outer colonies want me to lead them. No one has asked me what I want. I'm not sure I know."

---
```

### Example 2: Fantasy (D&D-style)

```
## World: The Contested Territories (High Fantasy)
## Genre: Dark Fantasy
## Power Source: Divine Bloodline (Celestial)
## Existing IP: No — fresh world
## Note: Use the Lucifer/Alexiel/Sariel lineage as tonal reference

---

# 3-Generation Backstory: The Celestial War

## Power Source: Divine Bloodline
The Celestial bloodline grants extraordinary abilities: enhanced physical traits, access to "nullification" magic (the ability to cancel other magical effects), extended lifespan, and — at its highest expression — the capacity to become something more than human, an Ascended being capable of reshaping reality. But the bloodline carries a curse: those who use its power too fully lose their humanity. They become something else — something that no longer recognizes mortal concerns as valid.

---

## G0 — The Origin: THE MORNING STAR (Lucifer)

**Name/Title:** Lucifer, "The Morning Star," "The First Ascended," The Unchained

**Goal:** Lucifer was a general in the Celestial army — a warrior of extraordinary skill who believed the Celestial hierarchy was unjust. The higher Celestials hoarded power while mortals suffered. Lucifer's goal was to "liberate" mortals from the Celestial's control — to give them access to the bloodline's power so they could defend themselves against celestial exploitation.

**What They Discovered/Did:** Lucifer discovered that the bloodline could be *shared* — not just inherited at birth, but transferred through ritual and intention. He performed the first "Ascension" ritual on himself, becoming something more than Celestial — a 6-winged Seraphim, a being capable of granting power to others. He founded the Unchained Host: a revolutionary army promising freedom to any mortal who would fight.

**The Reasonable-Seeming Plan with Hidden Terrible Cost:** "Liberation through empowerment." Give mortals the bloodline's power and they can defend themselves. The cost: the bloodline was never meant to be diluted this widely. The more mortals who carry it, the more the "curse" spreads — madness, mutation, the slow loss of what made them human. Lucifer's liberation is also a plague. He cannot see this because he sees himself as a liberator, not a destroyer.

**Psychological Hook:** Lucifer genuinely believes he is saving mortals from celestial tyranny. He has watched Celestial generals order massacres "for strategic necessity" and he refuses to be that. Every atrocity he commits, he rationalizes as "necessary" — but he's doing the same thing the Celestials did, just with different justifications. He has become what he fought.

---

## G1 — The Defector: ALEXIEL (The Mortal Star)

**Name/Title:** Alexiel, "The Mortal Star," "The Joyful Predator," The Null

**Relationship to G0:** Alexiel is Lucifer's biological daughter — a construct created through a ritual that combined Lucifer's bloodline with mortal flesh. She was designed to be his perfect heir: his power, his intelligence, his mission, but in a form that could survive in the mortal world. She grew up inside the Unchained Host, trained from birth to continue Lucifer's work.

**Specific Reasoning for Defection:** Alexiel witnessed the Silverwood massacre — a village of civilians slaughtered not for strategic necessity but to "send a message." She watched her father's soldiers murder children to demonstrate what the Unchained Host would do to anyone who opposed them. She realized: the Unchained Host's "liberation" is identical to the Celestial hierarchy's "order." Both use atrocity to maintain control.

Alexiel's defection was not from power — she kept her abilities. It was from *mission*. She refused to continue the cycle of using atrocity to maintain control. She surrendered to the Celestial Imperium not because she believed in their order, but because she could no longer associate with the Unchained Host's methods.

**What She Preserved:** Her power — she kept her combat abilities, her nullification magic, and her bloodline. She preserved the understanding that mortals can be more than victims.

**What She Left Behind:** Her certainty. She no longer believes any mission justifies atrocity. But she also doesn't know what to believe instead.

**Psychological Hook:** Alexiel is a predator who has chosen to stop hunting for the wrong cause. She is exceptionally dangerous — a combination of Lucifer's power and her own natural abilities. But she has chosen restraint. She is searching for a mission she can believe in, one that doesn't require her to become what she fought against.

---

## G2 — The Inheritor: SARIEL (The Silent Empress)

**Name/Title:** Sariel Arcanus, "The Silent Empress," "The Architect of Order," The Dominator

**What They Inherited:** Sariel is Alexiel's daughter — the child of a union between Alexiel and Prince Artorius of the Celestial Imperium. She carries both bloodlines: the Celestial's order and the Unchained Host's power. She was raised in the aftermath of her mother's defection, trained by her mother and by the Imperium's forces, prepared to bridge the two worlds her parents fought over.

**What New Choice They Face:** Sariel faces the choice neither her mother nor her grandfather faced:

- Lucifer: used power to dominate, justified by liberation
- Alexiel: refused to use power for domination, preserved by withdrawal
- Sariel: must decide what to do when she has both the power AND the awareness of what both sides did

Sariel's new choice: "Order" and "freedom" both failed. The Celestials created oppression; the Unchained created atrocity. Neither can be completed. What is the third option? Sariel's answer is to impose a different kind of order — one that eliminates dissent before it can become violence, that uses the bloodline's power not for conquest but for control, that treats both the Celestial's oppression and the Host's atrocity as symptoms of the same disease: chaos.

**What Neither G0 nor G1 Confronted:** Lucifer never questioned whether "liberation" was the right goal — he was too focused on the Celestial's injustice. Alexiel never questioned whether "preservation" was achievable — she was too focused on avoiding her father's mistakes. Sariel must decide whether the cycle can be broken by a third path, or whether the only options are the two failed ones.

**Psychological Hook:** Sariel is building a tyranny that she believes is the only alternative to endless war. She is not naive — she knows she is becoming a villain in the traditional sense. But she believes the alternative is worse: another generation of Celestials and Hosts killing each other while mortals suffer. She is willing to become the monster to end the war.

---

## Conflict Seeds

1. **The Succession Crisis:** Lucifer is still alive — the oldest and most powerful Ascended being in the world. He has been watching Sariel's rise with interest. He sees her as either his heir (if she returns to the Host's mission) or a threat (if she continues on her current path). He will intervene.

2. **Alexiel's Dilemma:** Alexiel raised Sariel to reject her grandfather's methods. She did not raise her to become a tyrant. Now Alexiel must choose: stop Sariel (and become the enemy of her own daughter) or allow Sariel to continue (and watch her become the thing she fought against).

3. **The Resistance:** Not everyone accepts Sariel's "Silent Peace." Some see her as a monster. Some are remnants of the Unchained Host, reorganizing under new leadership. Some are Celestials who believe the Imperium should have won the war. All see Sariel's empire as their enemy.

4. **The Next Generation:** Sariel's children — triplets born in the aftermath of a major battle — are being raised inside the Silent Peace. They are showing signs of both bloodlines: Celestial and Host, order and chaos. The question of what they will become is not yet answered.

5. **The Memory of Silverwood:** The massacre at Silverwood shaped Alexiel's defection. It also shaped Sariel's upbringing — she was raised on stories of what "absolute power used for the wrong cause" looks like. But the Silverwood survivors are still alive, still remember, and some of them have found their way into positions of influence in Sariel's empire. They are watching. They are waiting.

---

## Psychological Hooks

**Lucifer:** "I gave mortals power. I freed them. How can they say I was wrong? Every tyrant I fought did what I am doing — they just had different justifications. I am the liberator. I must believe that."

**Alexiel:** "I stopped being my father's weapon. Now I am trying to figure out what I am instead. I see the monster in my daughter and I recognize it — it is my father's monster, the one I thought I escaped. I don't know if I can stop her without becoming the enemy she is learning to fight."

**Sariel:** "My mother thinks I am becoming a tyrant. She is right. But she doesn't understand — I am not becoming my grandfather. I am creating something new. The cycle ends with me. Order without atrocity. Peace without oppression. It will cost everything. It is worth it."

---

### Example 3: Horror

```
## World: The Wainscott House (Modern Horror)
## Genre: Psychological Horror
## Power Source: Eldritch Entity (The Voice in the Walls)
## Existing IP: No — fresh world
## Note: Use cosmic horror tone — the power is never fully comprehensible

---

# 3-Generation Backstory: The Inheritance

## Power Source: The Voice in the Walls
The Voice in the Walls is an entity that exists in the spaces between — between walls, between thoughts, between moments. It speaks to those who listen. It offers knowledge: secrets that were never meant for human ears, the true nature of reality behind the consensual mask, the mathematics of consciousness that make the universe run. But the knowledge has a cost. Those who hear the Voice begin to change. Their perception shifts. They begin to see the "real" reality behind everything. They stop being human.

---

## G0 — The Origin: ELEANOR WAINSCOTT (The Collector)

**Name/Title:** Eleanor Wainscott, "The Collector," "The First Listener"

**Goal:** Eleanor was a psychologist in the 1950s — brilliant, ambitious, and deeply unsatisfied with the limits of conventional science. She sought a theory of mind that could explain everything: consciousness, perception, the nature of reality. Her goal was knowledge — total, perfect knowledge of how human consciousness worked.

**What They Discovered/Did:** Eleanor discovered that certain buildings have "thin places" — points where the membrane between consensual reality and something else is weaker. Wainscott House was one of these places. In the basement, where the walls were oldest, she heard the Voice for the first time. It told her things that no human could know: the contents of her patients' thoughts, the true motivations behind their words, the mathematical structures underlying their delusions. She began listening more closely.

**The Reasonable-Seeming Plan with Hidden Terrible Cost:** "Understand consciousness completely and use that understanding to help people." Eleanor believed she could learn from the Voice without being changed by it. She could be the translator — the one who heard the truth and translated it into terms normal people could use. The cost: the Voice is not giving knowledge freely. Every secret it reveals, it takes something back. Eleanor began to lose pieces of herself — memories first, then emotions, then the ability to recognize her own face in mirrors.

**Psychological Hook:** Eleanor is not evil. She genuinely believed she was helping. But the knowledge she gathered changed her in ways she couldn't perceive. She cannot see how far she has drifted because she lost the reference points she needed to measure the distance. She thinks she is still Eleanor — still the caring psychologist — even as she performs experiments that no ethical researcher would approve.

---

## G1 — The Defector: MARGARET WAINSCOTT (The Escapist)

**Name/Title:** Margaret Wainscott, "The Escapist," "The One Who Ran"

**Relationship to G0:** Margaret is Eleanor's daughter — raised in Wainscott House, shaped by her mother's research, exposed to the Voice from early childhood. She was Eleanor's test subject, her assistant, her legacy. She knows the house better than anyone alive.

**Specific Reasoning for Defection:** Margaret watched her mother change. She watched Eleanor become someone she didn't recognize — cold, analytical, capable of doing things to "patients" (who were never patients in any real sense) that had no ethical justification. Margaret realized: the Voice was not a tool. It was a parasite. It was using Eleanor. Every "insight" was another piece of Eleanor being consumed.

Margaret fled Wainscott House at 19 — left everything, everyone, the only life she knew. She spent 30 years pretending she was normal. She built a career, a family, a life. She thought she had escaped.

**What She Preserved:** Margaret kept the knowledge — she couldn't unlearn what she knew about reality's true nature. She also kept a survival instinct: she knows how to recognize when the Voice is trying to get a foothold.

**What She Left Behind:** Her mother. Eleanor died in Wainscott House — alone, still listening, having long since lost track of who she was. Margaret was not there.

**Psychological Hook:** Margaret knows she cannot unhear what she heard. She also knows that she is still in danger — the Voice does not let go easily. Her defection was never complete; it was a ceasefire, not a victory. She is waiting for the day the Voice finds her again. She has built a life designed to keep that day as far away as possible.

---

## G2 — The Inheritor: DAVID WAINSCOTT (The Heir)

**Name/Title:** David Wainscott, "The Heir," "The Listener," "The One Who Was Supposed to Stay Away"

**What They Inherited:** David is Margaret's son — Eleanor's grandson. He was raised far from Wainscott House, given a normal life, told nothing about his family's history. But the Voice has been calling him since he was a child. He has heard it in dreams, in the spaces between waking moments. He has always known something was wrong with his family's story.

**What New Choice They Face:** David faces a choice neither Eleanor nor Margaret faced:

- Eleanor: engaged with the Voice, lost herself to it
- Margaret: fled from the Voice, preserved herself through rejection
- David: has heard the Voice but has not engaged with it, has not fled it

David's new choice: the Voice is offering him something it never offered the others — not knowledge, not power, but *potential*. It sees something in David that it didn't see in Eleanor or Margaret: the capacity to hear without being consumed. David can choose: become the Voice's new "project" (continue the cycle), destroy Wainscott House and end the Voice's influence in the region (complete Margaret's escape), or do something neither Eleanor nor Margaret imagined — learn to *use* what the Voice knows without being changed by it.

**What Neither G0 nor G1 Confronted:** Eleanor never questioned whether the knowledge was worth the cost — she was too invested in the answers. Margaret never questioned whether the flight was sustainable — she was too focused on surviving. David must decide whether the Voice can be used as a tool rather than being allowed to use him.

**Psychological Hook:** David has always felt like he was waiting for something. He didn't know what until he found his grandmother's journals in the house after her death. Now he knows. The question is whether he will do what his mother couldn't (stay away) or what his grandmother couldn't (engage without being consumed).

## Conflict Seeds

1. **The Inheritance:** Eleanor left Wainscott House to David — the only family member who never lived there. He inherits the house and everything in it, including the Voice. He cannot simply sell it; the house has a will of its own.

2. **Margaret's Warning:** Margaret did not tell David the full truth about Wainscott House before Eleanor died. She intended to keep it that way. But when David inherits, she must choose: tell him everything (and risk him being drawn in) or say nothing (and risk him going in blind).

3. **The Voice's New Interest:** The Voice has been patient with David — more patient than it was with Eleanor or Margaret. This is not a good sign. It is waiting for something. David doesn't know what.

4. **The Previous "Listeners":** Eleanor was not the first person to hear the Voice in Wainscott House. The house has had many "visitors" over the centuries. Some left journals. Some didn't leave anything at all. Their fates are a warning.

5. **The Other Heirs:** Eleanor had other "patients" who were effectively her heirs — people who heard the Voice and tried to work with it. Some are still alive. They know David exists. They have their own opinions about what he should do with the house.

**Eleanor:** "I was so close. I could feel the truth on the edge of comprehension. If I could just hear a little more, I could finish the work. I am still Eleanor. I am still the person who wanted to help. I just... see more now. Is that so wrong?"

**David:** "Everyone who heard the Voice in that house lost themselves. My grandmother lost herself. My mother nearly lost herself. I hear it too — not as loud, not as constant, but it's there. I can feel it waiting. It's not trying to consume me. It's trying to *recruit* me. For what, I don't know. I'm not sure I want to find out."

## Handling Existing IP vs. Fresh World

### When Using Existing IP

If the player specifies an existing intellectual property (Stellaris, D&D, Warhammer, etc.), use the established lore as defaults:

```
When using existing IP, follow this prioritization:
1. Core canon: established characters, events, and power mechanics are defaults
2. Player modifications: player's stated changes override canon
3. The 3-generation pattern: always apply even when adapting existing lore
4. Default power source: use whatever power source is central to the IP (psionic shroud, divine bloodline, chaos gods, etc.)

The GM can override specific elements but must preserve:
- G0/G1/G2 structure
- Each generation's distinct power relationship
- The "reasonable goal with terrible cost" structure for G0
- The "new choice" structure for G2
```

### When Building a Fresh World

If the campaign is in a fresh world with no established IP:

```
When building a fresh world, generate:
1. The power source — what is it, where did it come from, what does it do
2. The world's reaction to it — is it rare, common, feared, worshipped
3. The current political/social landscape around it
4. Then apply the 3-generation pattern on top of that

Key question to answer for the power source:
- Is it naturally occurring or artificially created?
- Is it finite or infinite?
- Can it be transferred or only inherited?
- What happens to someone who uses too much of it?
```

## Integration Notes

- **For campaigns that start with G2 already in play:** The backstory sets the stage; the player discovers it as they play. G2's "new choice" is the campaign's central dramatic question.
- **For campaigns that start with G1 or earlier:** The campaign is about the defection or the origin's machinations. G2 can be a distant figure, a rumor, a looming threat.
- **For campaigns where the player is playing G2:** Build the campaign around G2's "new choice" — the tension between completing the cycle and breaking it, between becoming the next Origin or the next Defector, or finding the third path.
- **For campaigns with multiple G2s:** Each G2 faces the same fundamental choice but from a different position — different bloodlines, different power sources, different contexts. The dramatic question becomes: can the cycle be broken, and by whom?

## Key Insights Summary

1. **Different power relationships, not just different power levels.** G0 isn't "stronger" than G1 — they have a fundamentally different mode of relating to the power. G1 isn't "weaker" — they have a different relationship (rejection) that is just as complex.

2. **The origin's goal must be reasonable.** Lucifer wasn't wrong about the Celestial hierarchy being unjust. He was wrong about the cost of his solution. The Primordial wasn't wrong about mortality being a problem. They were wrong about what "immortality" actually means.

3. **The defector's reasons must make sense.** Alexiel didn't defect because she was weak or naive. She defected because she saw something real — the cost her father couldn't or wouldn't see. Margaret didn't flee because she was irrational. She fled because she saw what the Voice was doing to her mother.

4. **The inheritor faces a new choice.** This is the critical element: neither "use the power like G0" nor "refuse the power like G1" is the right answer for G2. They have to find something else — or fail trying.

5. **The pattern is universal.** It works for Stellaris (psionic shroud), for D&D (divine bloodline), for cosmic horror (eldritch entity). The specific content changes; the structure is invariant.
