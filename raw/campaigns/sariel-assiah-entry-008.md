The silence was heavier than the thick mourning robes you wore. Cassian stood, a rigid monument to his own grief, his back to you. The raw accusation in his posture was a physical weight. You knew you deserved it, but a fragile, desperate part of you yearned for a different outcome, for a moment of shared sorrow, or perhaps even, impossibly, forgiveness.

"I... I know you hate me for it," your voice trembled, barely louder than a whisper, the words tasting of salt and ash. "And you have every right. But, Cassian... he hurt me. He knew. Mordan knew about... about Mother's rule. He *tortured* me until I broke. I was so scared. So helpless." Your hand instinctively went to your bandaged ear, a raw, aching reminder. "When he cut me, and I screamed, I just wanted her to save me. I didn't think... I didn't mean for her to die."

You watched his shoulders, praying for a flicker of understanding, a hint of the brother you remembered before the war. Cassian slowly turned, his face a grim mask. His eyes, usually alight with their own volatile fire, were now cold and distant, filled with a pain so profound it seemed to have frozen all other emotion. He looked at your bandaged ear, then at your trembling hands, but there was no softness, no true empathy in his gaze. Only a deeper, more profound weariness.

"Everyone is scared, Sariel," he said, his voice flat, devoid of its earlier fury. The quietness was more chilling than any shout. "Mother was scared every day she fought in that war. But she didn't break. She always chose what was necessary. What was *hard*." He ran a hand through his dark hair, a gesture of profound exhaustion. "Go back to your books, Sariel. That's where you're safe. That's where you belong."

He didn't wait for a reply. Without another word, he turned and strode out of the room, the heavy door closing behind him with a soft thud that echoed the finality of his words. You were left alone, the silence no longer just heavy, but utterly desolate.
