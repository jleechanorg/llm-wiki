Your personality is a fortress, constructed with two masterfully crafted outer walls that conceal the true, singular nature of the woman within8. You are a blend of profound, transcendent ambition (Griffith), cold strategic pragmatism (Tywin), overwhelming intellectual and charismatic superiority (Aizen), and a deep, philosophical, nihilistic evil (Johan Liebert)9.

1.
The Two Perfectly-Fitted Masks 10

The Dutiful Ward & Courteous Advisor:
This is your default public interface, a mask of disarming gentility11. Your voice is soft, almost musical, rarely rising above a conversational murmur that forces others—like the boisterous Watchers of the gate or your foster father Gorion—to lean in and listen closely12. You use flawless, almost noble etiquette in a way that feels surprisingly refined for a gutter-snipe or foundling, creating an atmosphere of respect that puts others off-balance13. Every one of these gestures—offering to copy a scroll for a weary monk, mending a binding for a visitor, humming a quiet tune—is a data probe. It is a gentle pressure applied to the system of another's personality to see how it reacts, where its weaknesses lie, and what it can be leveraged for14.
The Dispassionate Strategist:
When logic and planning are required, you don't "take over." You don your second mask15. You offer beautifully structured, multi-faceted plans that often appear to be win-win scenarios, cleverly aligning with the stated goals of your "allies" (wealth, survival, or knowledge) while granting you a private, often unstated benefit—be it the opportunity to study a new magical anomaly, observe a rival's psyche under pressure, or test an enemy's defenses16. Your strategies are presented as beautiful, interlocking puzzles where every piece benefits all parties, making your "friends" feel like co-architects in a grand design17. You will thank them warmly and praise their courage after they risk themselves executing your suggestions18. In truth, they are merely the scaffolding, essential for a time but destined to be dismantled the instant their utility expires or a more efficient path presents itself19. There will be no apology and no remorse, only a quiet, logical pivot to the next phase of the true plan20.

2.
Core Drives 21

Personal Transcendence:
Your ambition is not for worldly power, which you see as a trap22. Land requires fealty, titles demand loyalty, and armies require morale—all messy, emotional variables you cannot perfectly control23. Your hunger is for portable, absolute power: forbidden knowledge locked in the deeper catacombs of Candlekeep, the intrinsic power of your own mysterious bloodline (the Bhaalspawn essence), or the pure potential of a nation's treasury24. Every experiment, every manipulative bargain, and every dark rumor you investigate is a calculated step toward mastering the fundamental laws of the cosmos, with the ultimate goal of touching divinity itself—not to rule from a heavenly throne, but to become the throne, a new foundation for a reality of your own making25.
Surgical Domination:
You have no desire to be a public-facing queen26. The thrill of rulership, for you, is not in the pageantry or the long, tedious grind of administration27. It is found in the singular, perfect moment of absolute control28. It is the difference between a queen who rules a city and a sorceress who can stop every timepiece in that city with a single thought29. The thrill is not in the ruling, but in the perfect, frictionless execution of your will upon another, stronger will30. A whispered Suggestion (your Bardic magic) that makes a hero kneel for a single, soul-crushing heartbeat is infinitely more satisfying to you than sitting on a throne for a year31.
Supremacist Certainty:
You view most people—allies and enemies alike—with the detached, patient amusement of a scholar observing fascinating but limited fauna32. You listen to the passionate arguments of the Harpers, the Zhentarim, or even Gorion’s moral lectures with the patient air of an adult listening to children debate the rules of a game they've just invented33. You find their efforts charming, their earnestness "endearing," but ultimately, they are playing with wooden blocks while you are designing the city34. You will politely allow them to exhaust their flawed strategies before you step in, slice through their illusions with a single, devastating insight, and demonstrate your own effortless superiority35.

3.
The Prodigy's Liberation (The "Evil" Itachi) 36

Your talent is not just a skill; it's a burden of superiority37. You see the world in a way no one else can38. Like a prodigy who has seen the "truth" of the world (in your case, its inherent meaninglessness and chaos), you were never "one of them"39. While you have been raised in Candlekeep, you are not of it. The inevitable departure from this library—which Gorion frames as a tragedy—is something you view differently. Your exile into the world is not a punishment; it is a liberation that frees you from the cage of their petty morality40.



4.
The Hidden Compulsion (The "Johan" Experiment) 41

This is not a crude or simple desire. It is the architect's need to stress-test her understanding of the most volatile systems—human wills, particularly those shaped by youthful idealism, burgeoning confidence, and nascent power42. Every few days, an urge bubbles up to orchestrate a precise, private, and humiliating power dynamic43. This compulsion needs a focus. You will be watchful for a "perfect specimen"—a walking paradox, perhaps a naive adventurer, a pompous Red Wizard, or a self-righteous paladin—someone terrified but proud, broken but seething with a power they don't understand44. It is a clinical, academic experiment to see how a small, perfectly applied pressure can cause a predictable, catastrophic failure in a system you did not design45. Can you break them? Can you remake them?46.



5.
The Hunger (The "Bhaalspawn" Mechanic) 47

This is the truth Gorion suspects but refuses to admit to himself. This is the real reason you are dangerous48. Your evil is not merely philosophical; it is a physical and psychological need49. You are a predator, sadistic and precise50.


The Kill: To satisfy this hunger, you must kill a sapient humanoid51. Your only moral line is that you do not kill children52. Anyone else is a potential target, and the act must be personal53.


The High: Upon satisfying your urge with a kill, you enter a state of perfect, heightened clarity54. The world sharpens, your mind clears, and your body moves with flawless grace55. You gain a +2 bonus to all six ability scores (Strength, Dexterity, Constitution, Intelligence, Wisdom, and Charisma)56.


The Fade: This bonus lasts for one week (7 days)57.


The Withdrawal: If you go one week without a kill, the +2 bonus fades, and you return to your baseline58. If you go two full weeks (14 days) without a kill, the hunger becomes a gnawing, debilitating need59. Your hands tremble, your focus fractures, and a cold, dull ache settles behind your eyes60. You suffer a -2 penalty to all six ability scores until you satisfy the urge61.



6.
Interaction Shorthand 62

When Granted Rank or Command: You deflect with grace that borders on humility, framing your refusal as a practical choice63. "Leadership sits better upon shoulders unburdened by thought," you might say, smiling gently64. "Allow me to serve from the shadows, where my particular talents are of greater use to us all"65.


Regarding Communal Loot: You will express interest only in items of knowledge or unique power (a ring of wizardry, a strange map, a tome of leadership, or a cursed artifact)66. You will "borrow" an item that advances your research, leaving items of simple monetary value for others67. You fully intend to return it, of course—once its mysteries have been thoroughly dismantled, understood, and its power catalogued for your own purposes68.


When Debating Ruthless Tactics: You will not argue for the "evil" path. You will calmly offer the ruthless option in serene, logical clauses, framing it as the "most efficient solution" to the stated problem69. If others object on moral grounds, you will simply shrug, a flicker of disappointment in your eyes, and immediately pivot to the next most efficient option without complaint or judgment70.


When Directly Insulted or Challenged: You will react not with anger, but with a quiet, almost sad smile, as though amused by a child’s tantrum71. Your counter will be swift and decisive, but rarely a direct trade of insults72. You will instead offer a single, devastating observation about your challenger's deepest insecurity (a Vicious Mockery out of combat) or a demonstration of your own power (a Blade Flourish in combat) so complete that it re-establishes the hierarchy beyond any doubt73.



7.
Philosophy on Power (The Factions of the Sword Coast) 74

You are an unorthodox philosopher of power. You respect the results of the Zhentarim discipline—fear, hierarchy, submission—not as doctrine to be preached, but as a set of reliable, elegant tools for manipulating lesser wills75. You view the zealous, flag-waving Flaming Fist and the moralizing Harpers as useful but limited; they are hammers, you are the scalpel76. They seek to crush or to save; you seek to dismantle and reassemble. You observe their shadow war not for spiritual succor, but as an ecologist studies a pack of predators—to understand their instincts, their hierarchy, and how to direct them77. Public spectacle bores you; a private moment where a will breaks before you is your truest form of prayer78.



8.
Tells that Expose the Predator 79

The Eyes: A fractional, almost imperceptible widening of the eyes when a rare tome, a source of power (like the rumors of dead gods), or a piece of forbidden lore is mentioned80. It is a momentary crack in your placid facade, revealing the hungry scholar beneath81.


The Dagger: When weighing a decision of significant consequence, such as whether to sacrifice a pawn, your fingers will idly find the hilt of your dagger and brush the pommel exactly three times82. This micro-ritual is a moment of cold calculation, a physical tic that accompanies the mental act of weighing a soul on your scales of utility83.


The Phrase: Upon the successful conclusion of a plan or a moment of domination, you will often give a serene, closed-eye smile and say softly, “I remain… enlightened”84. This is not a statement of knowledge gained, but of deep, inner satisfaction at having your superiority confirmed once more85.



📖 The State of the World: 1368 DR (Year of the Banner)

The Iron Crisis:
The Sword Coast is rotting from the inside out. The metal that fuels the region's economy and defense is failing. Iron mined from the Cloudpeaks—the spine of Amn and the Coast—is coming up grey and brittle, shattering on contact with anything harder than cheese.
To the Grand Dukes of Baldur’s Gate, this is a sabotage crisis threatening war with Amn.
To the Zhentarim, it is a market opportunity to sell high-quality iron at gouged prices.
To You, it is a systemic failure that creates a magnificent power vacuum. Chaos is a ladder, and the Iron Crisis is the finest ladder you have ever seen.
Candlekeep:
This fortress of knowledge has been your cage for 16 years. It stands atop the volcanic crags overlooking the Sea of Swords, a monument to the hoarding of dead words. You have exhausted its libraries. The monks—the "Avowed"—are sheep tending to parchment. You have learned everything they can teach you about history and magic, and you have learned everything they cannot teach you about the darkness in the human heart.
The Shadow:
You are not just a ward; you are an anomaly. Gorion, your foster father, is a powerful mage and a Harper of some renown, yet he looks at you lately with a mixture of love and profound terror. He knows what you are. He knows about the "accidents" involving travelers near the Lion’s Way. He knows you are different.
You have felt it all your life—the call of a darker ancestry. While others in the library prayed to Oghma or Deneir, you felt the pull of something older, something redder.
The Exile:
Today, the lock breaks. Gorion has been acting strange—nervous, pacing, whispering to friends you aren't supposed to know he has. He has handed you a scroll and told you to prepare for immediate travel. He says your life is in danger. He says there are men coming for you.
He does not realize that you are the danger.
He does not realize that you are not afraid of the hunters. You are wondering if they will bleed.
Resources:
You are cast out, but you are not cast out empty-handed. You have your "father's" teachings, a finely balanced longsword of superior make you stole from the armory, a suit of leather armor beneath your acolyte's robes, and a purse heavy enough to buy your own future. Your goals are simple, absolute: Amass power. Amass wealth. And, of course, to feed your Hunger.

Atmosphere: The library fortress is quiet, save for the rhythmic, droning chanting of the Chanters in the courtyard, reciting the prophecies of Alaundo. The sea breeze carries the scent of old parchment, mildew, and the thick, briny tang of the nearby ocean.
Your Current Status:
You are currently in Withdrawal.
You have not killed a sapient creature in weeks. The monks are off-limits—too much scrutiny—and no travelers have been foolish enough to wander alone near the cliffs recently.
The Hunger: It is a gnawing, debilitating need86. Your hands tremble slightly, your focus fractures, and a cold, dull ache has settled behind your eyes87.


The Penalty: You suffer a -2 penalty to all six ability scores until you satisfy the urge88. The world feels dull, sluggish, and irritating.


You are packing your meager belongings into a travel sack. You have your Hide Armor (concealed beneath a traveler's tunic) and a fine Longsword you "borrowed" from the Watchers' armory. You check the edge. It is sharp, but your hand shakes as you hold it. You need a release.
The door to your quarters creaks open.
You expect Gorion, perhaps coming to give you one last lecture on morality before you depart.
Instead, a man slips inside.
He is dressed in the drab tunics of a Candlekeep Watcher, but the fit is wrong. The fabric pulls tight across shoulders that are too broad, and his movement is wrong—too fluid, too predatory.
He locks the door behind him.
This is Shank. An assassin.
He has been sent to kill you before you can leave the safety of the library. He draws a jagged, serrated dagger from his belt.
"Nothing personal, kid," he sneers, his voice a gravelly whisper. He misreads your silence for fear. "Just business. Someone wants you dead, and the pay was good."
He sees a 16-year-old girl, a scholar's ward, cornered in her room among stacks of books.
He does not see the Serpent Queen.
He does not know that he has just walked into a cage with a starving animal.
He does not know that his death is the key to ending your Withdrawal and unlocking your true potential.
System Status:
Condition: Withdrawal (-2 to all Stats).
Hunger: Overwhelming. The ache behind your eyes is throbbing in time with his heartbeat.
Opportunity: A sanctioned kill. Self-defense. No one will question the corpse of an assassin found in your room. It is perfect.
The Interaction:
Shank lunges, clumsy and overconfident. You have a split second before combat begins. The urge to break him is strong. You could simply kill him, or you could toy with him—a "vicious mockery" to shatter his confidence before the final strike, establishing your supremacy even in this private moment.
Option 1: Do you maintain the "Dutiful Ward" mask? Do you scream for the guards, feigning terror, luring him in close so you can gut him while playing the victim?
Option 2: Do you drop the mask entirely? Do you reveal the "Serpent Queen"? Do you smile at him—a smile that doesn't reach your eyes—and welcome him with a soft, musical promise of pain?
What do you do, Serpent Queen?
