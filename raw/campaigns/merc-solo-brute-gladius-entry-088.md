The Stonehand dwelling was now a quiet charnel house, its occupants silenced, their final, raw despair a potent offering to Gladius's senses. The limp bodies of the parents lay still, their wills utterly shattered, their simple strength dissolved into pathetic, quivering silence. Their child, a tragic, lifeless bundle on the floor, was the ultimate proof of their shattered defiance. A cold, profound satisfaction settled deep in Gladius’s gut, a deep hum of absolute dominance. This was the pinnacle of his art, the complete erasure of lives, preceded by the unraveling of their very spirit.

The dawn, painting vibrant oranges and purples on the eastern sky, illuminated the grim tableau. Gladius, his face impassive, turned from the scene. It was time for Corvan, the sniveling envoy, to shoulder the true burden of Lord Silvanus's contempt. He moved to the nearest window, pushing aside a grimy curtain just enough to peer out. The Rusty Flagon, a dark, squat shape against the brightening sky, was visible in the distance. He raised his hand, shielding a small flint and steel, and with a quick, practiced motion, struck a single, sharp spark. It flared for a heartbeat, a tiny, orange pinprick against the pre-dawn gloom, then vanished. A subtle signal, easily missed by the average eye, but for Corvan, a silent, undeniable command to begin the grim cleanup. Gladius felt a tightening in his chest, a subtle surge of pleasure from this remote exertion of control, forcing the 'elite' to wallow in the filth of their own making.

With the signal sent, Gladius turned his attention to the dwelling itself. The Miller and Blackwood homes had yielded little, but his pragmatic mind, ever hungry for acquisition, demanded a thorough sweep here. He moved methodically, his Shadow-Step Boots whispering against the stone floor, his heavy splint mail a near-silent presence. He wasn't just searching for gold; he was searching for anything, any small possession, that would confirm the utter insignificance of these lives, to claim it as a final, petty trophy.

He ran his large hands over shelves, emptied crude wooden drawers, peered into dusty corners. The dwelling was simple, its few possessions worn and utilitarian. He found a small, iron-bound strongbox tucked beneath a loose hearthstone, a familiar hiding place. With a grunt of effort, he pried it open. Inside, he found a modest sum: thirty gold pieces, along with a few copper and silver. More than the Millers, less than substantial, but it was theirs, and now it was his. He swept the coins into his pouch. There was nothing else of note, just more of the mundane, the pathetic remnants of lives now erased. His disdain solidified; their defiance had been their ruin, their meager possessions merely added to his growing hoard.

The job was done. Eleven souls erased, three families silenced. The full light of dawn would soon break, and Greenmeadow would wake to an eerie, unnatural quiet.
