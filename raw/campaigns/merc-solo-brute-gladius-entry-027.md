Corvan’s whimpers had died, replaced by a shuddering silence. He sat frozen, utterly broken, a stark contrast to the calculating envoy who had entered the tavern less than an hour ago. Gladius savored the moment, the raw, delicious sensation of a "superior" being reduced to a terrified puppet. The sun was now fully over the horizon, casting pale light through the grimy windows, revealing the full extent of Corvan’s pale, sweat-streaked face.

"Listen closely," Gladius commanded, his voice a low, gravelly whisper that nonetheless carried absolute authority. He leaned in, forcing Corvan to meet his gaze. "The bodies. They go into the marsh. Deep. Weighted. You will ensure no trace is left. No blood, no dragged marks, nothing for a hound to track. You will burn their bedding, their clothes, anything that holds a scent. You will use the village well to wash the surfaces clean. And any valuables, anything worth anything, you will bring to me. Understand?" Each instruction was a blunt, cold hammer blow, stripping away Corvan's last vestiges of dignity, binding him inextricably to the grim task.

Corvan swallowed hard, his eyes wide, reflecting Gladius's grim face like a distorted mirror. He looked as if he might vomit, but he merely nodded, a jerky, desperate movement. His expensive silks seemed to mock him, now stained with the metaphorical mud of Gladius's will. "Yes... yes, I understand," he choked out, his voice hoarse, raw with terror. He was beyond resistance, beyond thought. He was merely an instrument.

Gladius straightened, a grim satisfaction settling deep in his gut. This was more than just a contract; it was a personal victory, a delicious taste of the power he craved. The peasants were merely the fuel for this exquisite exercise in control. "Good," he grunted, a final, dismissive word. He turned on his heel, his new Shadow-Step Boots barely whispering against the floorboards, leaving Corvan to his terror and his impending task. The Miller farmhouse, with its rare herbs and oblivious inhabitants, awaited.
