The echo of the closing door, heavy and final, sealed you in a fresh wave of desolation. Cassian’s words, cold and sharp, cut deeper than any blade. You were the reason. The greatest commander in the world, the legendary hero, had fallen because you, a mere scholar, had failed. The guilt was a physical weight, pressing down on your chest.

Seeking solitude now felt like a surrender to the crushing despair. You needed to act, to escape the suffocating silence of your own culpability. Valerius. Perhaps he, with his calm logic, would offer something other than raw accusation. Or at least, his silence would be less condemning than Cassian's.

You rose from the bed, your legs feeling heavy, and moved towards the door. The familiar sounds of the bustling Spire outside your suite, once a comforting background hum, now felt distant and muted, as if the world outside was continuing without you.

You found Valerius not in the sparring chambers, nor in the strategic war room, but in his personal study – a minimalist space of polished obsidian and glowing data-slates, devoid of any personal touches. He sat at a large, central desk, a dozen reports spread meticulously before him. He was dressed in the simple, unadorned uniform of a High Commander, devoid of any of his usual ceremonial embellishments. His face was a mask of cold, analytical silence, his Starlit Eyes fixed on a holographic projection of the Syrn Valley, its terrain marked with grim, precise casualty reports. He did not look up as you entered.

The room, like the man himself, radiated a chilling, almost sterile calm, far more unsettling than Cassian's raw fury. Here, grief was not expressed; it was processed.

You stood for a long moment, unsure how to break the silence. His meticulous focus, the utter absence of any outward emotion, was intimidating. He finally sighed, a soft, almost imperceptible sound of profound weariness, and waved a hand, dismissing the holographic map. He looked up then, his gaze cool and direct, devoid of the volatile fire of Cassian's eyes, but holding a different, colder judgment.

"Sariel," he said, his voice flat, a tone of detached observation. "You are not usually one for the morning reports. Has Cassian finally left you in peace?"
