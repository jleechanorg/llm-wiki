Cressida’s words, a gentle but firm command, had planted a seed of purpose in the barren landscape of your grief. To honor Mother, to prove worthy of her sacrifice, you had to understand her, truly understand the legend that had shaped your world and then shattered it. The truth. That was your blade.

You left Cressida's chambers, the weight on your shoulders feeling marginally lighter, replaced by the unfamiliar hum of determination. Your feet carried you almost instinctively through the gleaming corridors of the Spire, descending into the cool, quiet depths of the Great Archives. This was your sanctuary, a place where the clamor of the court and the accusations of your brothers faded into the rustle of ancient parchment. The air here smelled of dust, drying ink, and the profound, comforting stillness of forgotten knowledge.

You sought out the hushed, cavernous sections dedicated to the Host Wars, the era that had forged your mother’s legend. Ignoring the gilded, public-facing monuments to Imperial triumph, you began a meticulous search for the more obscure records: raw field reports, logistical analyses, the terse, unedited accounts from the early days of her defection. You bypassed the grand narratives of "The Mortal Queen," seeking the granular data of "The Commander." Your hands, usually deft with star charts, now moved with a quiet, desperate precision, sifting through hundreds of scrolls and data-slates, your mind a relentless engine of inquiry. Each official omission, each heavily redacted passage, was a new clue, a new challenge to your intellect. You felt a cold, familiar satisfaction in solving these puzzles, a fleeting balm against the gnawing guilt. You were not just reading history; you were dissecting a life, searching for the true answers to its devastating end.
