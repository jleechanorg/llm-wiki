The air was growing colder as Gladius approached the cluster of Blackwood cottages. The memory of the Millers, so easily snuffed out, still hummed within him, a satisfying echo of exerted power. His previous assessment of the Blackwoods – isolated, independent – had been based on the lackey's words, but Gladius trusted only his own observations.

He veered off the main path, his Shadow-Step Boots absorbing the slight crunch of frosted grass and damp earth beneath his feet. The metallic whisper of his splint mail was barely a sound in the profound stillness of the pre-dawn hour. He moved with a practiced, predatory caution, circling the small cluster of three decrepit dwellings. Moonlight, thin and watery, cast long, distorted shadows, making the cottages appear like hunched, skeletal figures against the backdrop of the marsh.

As he moved, his senses drank in the environment. The scent of stagnant water from the nearby stream mingled with the faint, almost imperceptible smell of smoke from a long-dead hearth. The cottages themselves seemed to lean inward, their windows like sunken eyes, utterly dark and uninviting. There were no signs of activity, no flickering lamps, no hint of stirring life. The lackey had called them "fiercely independent," and Gladius could feel it in the pervasive silence, the subtle defiance in their utter lack of preparedness. They seemed to trust only in their isolation, their own self-reliance.

*Weakness,* Gladius concluded, a subtle, cold satisfaction in his gut. Their independence was merely a façade, a self-delusion against the inevitable. There were no watch dogs, no locked gates, no obvious traps. Just thin wooden walls and the vulnerable flesh within. The Blackwoods, in their stubborn self-sufficiency, had simply made themselves easier targets. He cataloged the easiest points of entry – a loose shutter, a sagging doorframe. It would be a simple, clean sweep. The experiential pleasure of the hunt, the tangible reality of his overwhelming power, was a raw, immediate satisfaction.
