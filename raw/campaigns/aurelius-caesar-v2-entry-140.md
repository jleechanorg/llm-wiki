For eight hours, you have stood as a silent sentinel, the Aegis of Julia braced against the wind. The small talk with Aurelia was a fragile thing—you spoke of the way the sun hits the marble in the Forum at dawn, a world away from this grit, and she listened with an intensity that suggested she was memorizing your voice for a future she wasn't sure you'd see. When the relief shift finally arrived, you were dismissed to the lower galleries, your limbs heavy and your armor weeping salt-water.

But as you seek the sanctuary of sleep in the deeper caves, the Command Grotto erupts in a storm that has nothing to do with the mountain weather. You are not present, but your name—and the weight of your deeds—fills the stone chamber.

"You are fools!" Naevia’s voice, once a broken whisper in the salt-pits, now rings with a terrifying, crystalline fury. She stands before Spartacus and Crixus, her skin glowing with the unnatural health of your divine touch, her finger stabbing the air toward the Western Cleft. "I sat in the dark for years, waiting for a god or a devil to find me. And when he came, he did not ask for my tribe or my worth. He gave his own life—three separate times! At the gate, at the pillar, and on those Spires—he stood in the path of the grave so we could walk!"

Crixus paces like a caged beast, his hand white-knuckled on his gladius. "He is a Caesar, Naevia! The very blood is a poison!"

"The poison is in your head, Gaul!" Naevia roars, stepping into his space, her eyes unblinking. "You stand here debating the 'politics' of his father while a boy of sixteen summers—a boy barely out of boyhood!—bleeds his hands raw hauling stone for your pride? If he wanted us dead, he could have signaled the legions from the ridge. Instead, he became a sun in the dark so your 'Undefeated' heart wouldn't have to break over my corpse."

Aurelia steps forward, her silvered scars catching the torchlight. Her voice is lower, but it carries the resonant weight of an oath. "He has saved my life three times already. In the square, in the sewers, and on these slopes. I have seen his eyes, Spartacus. He doesn't seek a throne. He seeks a death that means something because he is too weary to carry the shame you keep piling on him."

She looks Spartacus directly in the eye, her defiance absolute. "You call yourselves leaders of the free, yet you treat the only Roman who remembers how to be human like a beast of burden. If you break him with this 'ridiculous' penance, you lose more than a soldier. You lose the soul of this mountain."

Spartacus remains motionless, the firelight dancing in his unreadable gaze. The silence that follows is a physical weight, heavier than the limestone of the Cleft, as the two women stand as a shield for the boy who refused to be a Prince.
