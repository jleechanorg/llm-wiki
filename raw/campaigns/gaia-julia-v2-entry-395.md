The morning sun of Rome does not merely shine upon the Grand Audience Hall; it seems to pool around your throne, turning the translucent Tyrian silk of your gown into a shimmering bruise of liquid light. Beside you, your father, Caesar the Elder, reclines with the weary, proprietary grace of a Titan. The heavy bronze doors groan open, admitting the scent of the dusty Appian Way and a group of mortals whose worlds are about to tilt on their axes.

Batiatus and Lucretia lead the procession, their backs rigid with the practiced humility of those entering the Palatine. But as Batiatus raises his head to offer his first genuflection, his gaze hits you. He stops so abruptly that his sandals screech against the marble. His jaw unhinges, his oily complexion draining into a shade of deathly grey. He looks at your father, then at the gold laurel diadem resting upon your dark curls, then back to your wide, 'girlish' smile.

"L-Lady... Vibia?" Batiatus stammers, his voice a pathetic, high-pitched scrape. "My Lord Consul... why is... why is the provincial girl sitting where a goddess should?"

Lucretia's hand flies to her throat, her knuckles white as she realizes the scale of the 'novelty' she tried to claim in Capua. She doesn't speak; she simply shivers in the warm morning light, her eyes dilated with the bone-deep realization that she has been playing games with a wildfire.

Gaius Claudius Glaber strides in behind them, his crimson cape caught in the draft. He halts beside Batiatus, his face a map of absolute cognitive dissonance. He stares at you, the vein at his temple throuding in a frantic, rhythmic beat. Unlike the Lanista, his confusion is sharpened by a soldier's instinct for danger.

"Consul," Glaber rasps, his voice a jagged blade of military dread. "I was summoned to give account... but I find I am at a loss. Why is this... this child, this Gaia Vibia of the dust-veins, seated in the shadow of the Eagle? Has the Julian house grown so fond of its provincial toys that it seats them beside the Sword of Rome?"

You let out a sudden, delighted trill of laughter—the bell-like chime of 'Gaia Vibia'—and reach out with a gold-ringed hand to playfully pat your father's arm. "Oh, Gaius Claudius! You're still so dreadfully serious!" you chirp, your eyes wide and vacant of anything but 'reckless' joy. "I told you I had more important things to do than watch men sweat in the sand! Father, isn't he funny? He thinks I'm a toy!"

Caesar the Elder offers a weary, indulgent smile, his hand resting affectionately over yours. He looks at Glaber with the clinical boredom of a man observing a particularly slow beetle. "This 'child', Praetor, is Gaia Julia Caesar. My daughter. The wildfire of my house. If you found her in Capua... then you should be grateful you survived the encounter."

Behind the Praetor, the 'reward' has arrived. Spartacus stands amidst Crixus, Gannicus, and Oenomaus—chains that feel increasingly like a joke in this hall. The Thracian looks at you, his eyes locking onto yours with a sudden, predatory stillness. Unlike the Romans, he doesn't look confused; he looks like a man who has finally seen the sun and realized the cage was always made of light.
