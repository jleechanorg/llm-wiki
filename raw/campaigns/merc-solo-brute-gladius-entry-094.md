The village was truly waking now, the air filled with the scent of woodsmoke and damp earth. Gladius, his splint mail surprisingly muted by his Shadow-Step Boots, moved with the quiet menace of a predator among lambs. He had left Corvan to his grim, degrading task, the memory of the envoy's terror a potent, pleasing aftertaste. The final loose end, the bribed sheriff, beckoned.

He reached Borin’s home, a slightly larger cottage near the village well. The faint aroma of cheap ale, hinting at the sheriff's vice, emanated from a barrel near the door. Gladius felt a cold hum beneath his skin; this was the raw, immediate pleasure of confirmation, of seeing a supposed authority figure reduced to a pathetic, predictable weakness. He didn't knock. The door, left ajar, creaked inward at his touch, revealing a dimly lit interior thick with the cloying scent of stale alcohol and unwashed linen.

The common room was a mess: a overturned chair, a half-empty bottle of elven wine (the "potent vintage" Corvan had mentioned) on a table, a trail of spilled liquid leading to a back room. Gladius followed the scent, his disdain for such slovenliness a bitter taste in his mouth. He found Sheriff Borin sprawled across a cot in a small, unkempt bedroom, snoring loudly, his face flushed and slack with drunken stupor. A half-eaten meal, long cold, lay beside him. The man was completely insensible, utterly incapacitated. No threat, no witness, no complication.

Gladius simply stood over him for a moment, his immense shadow falling over the pathetic figure. There was no rage, only a cold, almost aesthetic appreciation for the purity of Borin's corruption, his easy pliability. The man, meant to uphold order, was a self-made prisoner of his own weakness. *Pathetic,* Gladius mused, a subtle sneer touching his lips. It was a tangible, visceral proof of how easily even the powerful could be bought, or simply drowned in their own vices. The grim satisfaction was profound, a final, quiet victory before he left this insignificant village behind.
