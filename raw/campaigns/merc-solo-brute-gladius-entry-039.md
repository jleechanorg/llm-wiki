`[Mode: STORY MODE]`
`Timestamp: 1492 DR, Ches 22, 02:40:00 AM`
`Location: Miller Family Farmhouse, Parents' Bedroom, Greenmeadow Village`

The cold, still forms of the Miller parents lay on their bed, their silent departure a testament to Gladius's brutal efficiency. The silent strangler's cord, still warm from its recent use, was a satisfying weight in his hand. He had found the meager twenty gold pieces, a trivial sum, but then his fingers had closed around the small, leather pouch, and a potent, sweet aroma had wafted forth. *Herbs.* The Miller's prized strain, the reason Lord Silvanus had orchestrated this petty, brutal affair.

His low Intelligence didn't mean his mind was dull; it simply meant his calculations were direct, pragmatic, and focused on raw power and acquisition. *These herbs,* he thought, turning the pouch over in his hand, feeling the dry, delicate leaves within. *Valuable in the city. To 'certain city figures'.* He remembered the dockworker's drunken lament, the tavern keeper's casual scoffing about Silvanus. The 'elite' of Baldur's Gate, the ones who thought their power was absolute, their desires unquestionable.

A grim, almost predatory smile touched Gladius's lips. Selling them for gold was the obvious path, a simple transaction. But the *knowledge* of these herbs, their *source*, the very secret of their cultivation... that held a different kind of value. *Could I make someone else learn their trade? Force them to reproduce this work?* The thought sent a jolt of pleasure through him, far more potent than the twenty gold pieces in his pocket. It wasn't just about selling a product; it was about controlling the supply, about holding a new kind of leverage over the wealthy, those who desired such potent concoctions. He pictured a city herbalist, perhaps a well-respected one, forced to come to *him* for supplies, or worse, forced to train others under his direction. The idea of these 'city figures' relying on him, an unwashed mercenary, to fulfill their desires, to bend to his will... that was the true prize. It would be a subtle, insidious form of dominance, a reflection of the control he’d just exerted over Corvan.

The risks? Being traced to the source, of course. But who would connect him, 'The Hammer,' a blunt instrument, with a delicate pouch of herbs? He scoffed internally. *They're too stupid, too arrogant.* He had the element of surprise, the cloak of night, and the willing accomplice of a terrified envoy. No, the potential leverage, the subtle power this represented, was far too great to simply hand over to a common merchant. He would keep the pouch. For now. It was a secret weapon, another thread in the web he was weaving around those who believed themselves untouchable.

His eyes, cold and satisfied, swept over the utterly still forms on the bed. The Miller family had served their purpose. Now, it was time for the next step.

`Timestamp: 1492 DR, Ches 22, 02:42:00 AM`
`Location: Miller Family Farmhouse, Parents' Bedroom, Greenmeadow Village`

The air in the Miller's bedroom, now thick with the scent of death and the cloying sweetness of the herbs in Gladius’s pouch, was still. The two parents lay motionless, their lives snuffed out with silent precision. Gladius stood over them, the Silent Strangler’s Cord still in his hand, a chilling extension of his will. He ran a hand over the rough-hewn wooden bedframe, his gaze sharp, taking in the mundane details of their lives – a worn blanket, a chipped ceramic mug on a bedside table, a small, faded painting of a distant mountain scene on the wall. These insignificant objects, once filled with their simple lives, now merely bore witness to their brutal end.

He tightened his grip on the cord, then carefully re-coiled it, returning it to his belt. The greatsword on his back felt like a loyal, silent companion, ready for the next phase. The thought of Corvan, waiting at the inn, a terrified puppet, brought a grim, almost gleeful satisfaction. It was time to pull his strings. Gladius felt a surge of raw power, a primal satisfaction in orchestrating the chaos, in making the powerful bend. The murder of these peasants was merely the first move in a grander game, a game he intended to win. The distant, faint orange glow of the burning shed was a silent promise of the havoc he was creating, a subtle signature in the darkness.

He took one last look at the extinguished lives, a final, dismissive glance. There was nothing more here. Only the quiet evidence of his passage, waiting for Corvan to erase. The night was still young, and there were seven more souls to claim, two more families to silence, before the dawn truly broke over Greenmeadow. The Blackwoods and the Stonehands. They would learn, just as the Millers had, that defying the will of those who wield true power was a fatal mistake.
