The Stonehand dwelling, now a silent, grim testament to Gladius's work, felt increasingly exposed as the false dawn gave way to the sharp, clear light of morning. The sun, a growing orange orb on the horizon, threatened to illuminate every detail, every chilling absence. Corvan, Gladius's terrified puppet, was taking too long. Impatience, a familiar prickle, began to itch beneath Gladius's skin. The job needed to be clean, and it needed to be done quickly. He would not leave loose ends, not even for the sake of savoring Corvan's humiliation. That would come later.

With a low grunt, Gladius began the grim work himself. He moved with a brutal practicality, his large hands surprisingly adept at the gruesome task. He dragged the limp bodies of the Stonehands across the stone floor, the silent tang of blood and fear filling the air. He located the heavy stones he’d noted earlier, remnants of a collapsed wall outside, and began the laborious process of weighting the corpses. His splint mail scraped against the stone, a rhythmic, guttural sound in the profound silence. There was no revulsion, only a cold, almost detached efficiency. This was simply another aspect of the contract, another physical task to master, another raw experience of control. The very act of cleansing, of removing the evidence, reinforced his power, his ability to erase and reshape reality according to his will.

Time bled away as he worked. The sun rose higher, its light pouring through the grimy windows, exposing the chilling tableau with increasing clarity. Just as he finished weighting the last body, preparing it for the marsh, a shadow fell across the open doorway. Corvan stood there, his face a sickly pale green, his silks disheveled, his eyes wide and bloodshot from a night of terror and forced waiting. He looked utterly wretched, a broken man. He stammered, "I... I came as fast as I could. The Rusty Flagon was... locked."

Gladius straightened, his vast shadow falling over the trembling envoy. He hadn't bothered to wash his hands; a faint smear of dark red on his gauntlets spoke volumes. His eyes, cold and piercing, fixed on Corvan, dismissing his pathetic excuse with a single, contemptuous glance. "Took you long enough," Gladius rumbled, his voice low, filled with a brutal, cutting disdain. "Thought I’d have to do it all myself. Are you even capable of following simple orders?" The unspoken accusation, the clear implication of Corvan’s inadequacy, hung heavy in the air. This was the moment of subtle, yet profound, dominance he had anticipated.
